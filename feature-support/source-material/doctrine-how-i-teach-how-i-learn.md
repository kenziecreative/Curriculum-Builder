# How I Teach. How I Learn. Why It Works.
_A personal philosophy of learning and teaching — organized for clarity._

---

> Metaskills are the foundation. The teaching and learning models are two sides of the same coin sitting on top of it. One governs how knowledge is delivered. The other governs how it is absorbed, transferred, and made useful.

---

## Part One: The Foundation

### The Six Metaskills

Everything else in this document rests on these six capacities. Metaskills aren't subject-matter knowledge — they're the underlying human abilities that determine how well you can acquire, apply, and adapt anything you learn. They don't become obsolete. They compound.

The premise is straightforward: most of the skills that matter most in work and life exist in wicked environments — domains where repetition alone doesn't produce mastery, where context shifts constantly, and where yesterday's expertise can become tomorrow's blind spot. In those environments, the people who win aren't necessarily the ones who know the most. They're the ones whose foundational capacities allow them to learn faster, pattern-match across domains, and rebuild their mental models when the rules change.

That's what metaskills do. They're not what you know. They're how well you can know.

| Metaskill      | What It Looks Like                                                          |
| -------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Exploring**  | Questioning assumptions, finding root causes, staying curious at depth      |
| **Creating**   | Breaking and reframing — the ability to make something new from what exists |
| **Feeling**    | Empathy, emotional intelligence, reading what isn't being said              |
| **Imagining**  | Visualizing what doesn't yet exist — futures, possibilities, scenarios      |
| **Innovating** | Generating novel solutions that work under real-world constraints           |
| **Adapting**   | Rebuilding mental models — staying fluid as context and complexity shift    |

These six aren't arbitrary categories. They map to the full range of human cognitive and emotional capacity — from the inward work of curiosity and empathy, to the outward work of innovation and adaptation. Together, they describe a person who can function well in complexity rather than just in clearly-defined, rule-based environments.

> Metaskills are the connective tissue. Every good teacher, every fast learner, every person who seems to thrive in ambiguity — look closely and you'll find these capacities operating underneath.

## Part Two: Two Models, One Coin

The teaching model and the learning model aren't separate philosophies. They're designed to work in tandem — one for how knowledge gets delivered, the other for how it gets internalized and extended. Understanding the difference matters because they answer different questions. The teaching model asks: how do I structure what I'm sharing so that someone can actually use it? The learning model asks: given new or complex material, how do I break it down fast enough to apply it without starting from scratch?

Neither model works well without the other. A well-structured lesson is only as useful as the learner's ability to make it their own. And a powerful personal learning approach still benefits from material that has been organized and scaffolded well.

---

### The Teaching Model: Theory → Method → Application

This is the delivery framework. It structures instruction to move a learner from abstract understanding to something they can actually do. The logic is sequential: you can't apply what you don't understand, and you can't structure your application without a method to follow.

|Stage|The Question|What It Does|
|---|---|---|
|**Theory**|The Why|Foundational concepts, principles, the logic beneath the skill|
|**Method**|The How|A systematic process or technique for putting the theory to work|
|**Application**|The Now What|Using the method to solve a real problem in a real context|

Theory establishes the conceptual foundation — the principles, frameworks, and underlying logic. Without this, learners are following steps they don't understand and will fail the moment conditions change. Method bridges theory and practice. It provides structure — a repeatable process for applying the theory — without locking the learner into a script. Application is where the real learning happens. It grounds everything in context, exposes gaps in understanding, and builds the muscle memory of actually doing.

This scaffolded progression moves learners from understanding to doing to transferring. It maps cleanly onto Bloom's Taxonomy, but the more important thing is that it respects how people actually learn: conceptual grounding first, structured process second, and open-ended application third.

> Teaching this way doesn't just deliver information. It implicitly builds the Metaskills. When you move someone from theory to application, you're training them to think, not just to execute.

---

### The Learning Model: Deconstruct → Draw Comparisons → Rebuild

This is the absorption framework. It's designed for the wicked environments where the 10,000-hour rule breaks down — where you don't have years to develop expertise through repetition, where domains shift faster than mastery can accumulate, and where the ability to transfer knowledge across contexts is more valuable than deep knowledge in any single context.

The insight behind this model is that experts in wicked domains don't start from scratch every time they face a new problem. They pattern-match. They pull from analogous experiences, deconstruct the new problem into familiar components, and rebuild a working model fast enough to act on it. This framework makes that process explicit.

| Stage                | The Move         | What It Does                                                                               |
| -------------------- | ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Deconstruct**      | Break It Down    | Identify the root structure — what are the real components beneath the surface complexity? |
| **Draw Comparisons** | Find the Pattern | What does this remind you of? Where have you seen this before in another domain?           |
| **Rebuild**          | Make It Yours    | Reconstruct the problem or concept using your existing mental models and new understanding |

Deconstruct is the Exploring metaskill in action — questioning assumptions, getting past surface complexity to underlying structure. Draw Comparisons is where the pattern recognition lives. It's the bridge between what you know and what you're trying to learn. The more domains you have experience in, the richer this step becomes. Rebuild is the Adapting metaskill — reassembling a working mental model that you can actually use, one that fits your existing knowledge and the new context simultaneously.

This isn't a linear process so much as a practiced orientation. People who are fast learners in complex environments tend to do these three things reflexively. The model just makes the habit explicit enough to teach.

> Deconstruct → Draw Comparisons → Rebuild is what Metaskills look like in action when someone faces a novel problem. It's not a trick. It's a disciplined orientation toward the unknown.

---

### How They Work Together

The teaching model gets knowledge into someone. The learning model helps them do something with it in situations you never anticipated.

|Teaching Model|Learning Model|
|---|---|
|How knowledge is delivered|How knowledge is internalized and extended|
|Theory → Method → Application|Deconstruct → Draw Comparisons → Rebuild|
|Moves learners from abstract understanding to concrete, usable skill through structured scaffolding|Enables rapid domain entry and knowledge transfer in wicked environments where repetition alone fails|

When you teach using Theory → Method → Application, you're giving people not just content but a structure for thinking about content. When they learn using Deconstruct → Compare → Rebuild, they're taking that structure and extending it — applying it laterally, adapting it to novel contexts, building on it rather than just repeating it.

## Part Three: The Delivery Architecture

### The Flipped Classroom

The flipped classroom is the deployment model that brings both frameworks to life in practice. In the traditional model, class time is spent delivering foundational content — lecture, explanation, curriculum. The flipped model inverts this. Foundational content is consumed beforehand, independently. Time with an instructor or expert is reserved for application, problem-solving, mentoring, and the kind of adaptive dialogue that can't happen passively.

This matters because the scarcest resource in any learning relationship isn't content — content is increasingly abundant. The scarcest resource is expert attention. The flipped model protects that resource by ensuring that live time isn't spent on things a video or reading could do just as well. It saves the conversation for what a conversation is uniquely good at: navigating complexity, responding to specific situations, and helping someone bridge the gap between knowing and doing.

**Traditional:** Instructor delivers content in session. Learner applies independently afterward, often without support.

**Flipped:** Learner engages with content independently beforehand. Session time is used for application, mentoring, adaptive guidance, and real-world problem-solving.

The flipped model doesn't change what is taught. It changes where the scarce resource — expert attention — gets spent.

The research on flipped learning is solid, particularly for adult learners. Adults tend to be self-directed, experience-driven, and pressed for time. The flipped model accommodates all three: they control the pace of foundational learning, bring their own experience to application sessions, and don't waste live time on content they could access independently.

> The flipped classroom isn't just a scheduling choice. It's a statement about where learning actually happens — not in the transfer of information, but in the guided application of it.

#### How It Maps to the Teaching Model

The flipped classroom is the structural expression of Theory → Method → Application. Theory and Method are what learners engage with independently before the session — through video, reading, frameworks, exercises. Application is what happens live, with expert support. The session becomes what it should always be: a place to practice, fail safely, get feedback, and connect learning to real situations.

This also means the instructor role shifts. Rather than being the source of content, the instructor becomes a facilitator, coach, and Socratic guide. The questions get better. The conversation gets richer. The learner arrives prepared, which means the session can go deeper and further than it otherwise could.

#### How It Maps to the Learning Model

When learners come to a session having already engaged with foundational content, they've already begun the Deconstruct phase. They've sat with the theory, noticed what they don't understand, and formed initial questions. The session is where Draw Comparisons and Rebuild happen — where the instructor helps them connect new concepts to existing knowledge and apply the framework to their actual situation. The learning model isn't something that happens after the session. It's activated by the structure of the flipped design.

## Part Four: The Complete Architecture

Put together, these three components form an integrated philosophy for how learning works and how to design for it. They operate at different levels of abstraction but reinforce each other continuously.

**Layer 1 — Foundation: The Six Metaskills** The capacity layer. What makes learning possible across contexts. Exploring · Creating · Feeling · Imagining · Innovating · Adapting

**Layer 2 — The Models: Teaching and Learning** The transfer layer. How knowledge moves between people and takes root. Teaching: Theory → Method → Application Learning: Deconstruct → Draw Comparisons → Rebuild

**Layer 3 — Delivery: The Flipped Classroom** The structural layer. How sessions are designed so expert time does what only expert time can do. Learner prepares independently. Live time used for application and adaptive mentoring.

---

The Metaskills are the bedrock. Without underlying capacity — curiosity, empathy, imagination, adaptability — the models are just procedures. With them, the models become second nature. The learner isn't following a framework; they're expressing a way of engaging with complexity.

The Teaching and Learning models are the engine. They describe what actually happens when knowledge moves: the deliberate scaffolding of a teacher who has thought hard about structure, and the disciplined orientation of a learner who knows how to enter new domains without starting from scratch.

The Flipped Classroom is the container — the design decision that creates the conditions for everything else. It protects what's scarce, activates what's already been built, and ensures that live time produces the thing that only live time can produce: adaptive, real-world application with expert support.

> The goal isn't knowledgeable people. It's capable people — people who can apply what they've learned, adapt it when conditions change, and keep learning after the formal teaching ends.
