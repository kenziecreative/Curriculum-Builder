# Phase 7: Metaskills in Curriculum Design — Operationalizing Foundational Capacities

## Summary of Findings

The doctrine positions the Six Metaskills (Exploring, Creating, Feeling, Imagining, Innovating, Adapting) as "the foundation" on which everything else rests. This phase investigated whether that positioning can survive operationalization — whether these capacities can function as active design constraints in a curriculum builder, not just as philosophical backdrop. The answer is yes, but with structural distinctions the doctrine currently collapses and an implementation model more complex than simple tagging.

---

## Finding 1: The Goals-vs-Principles Distinction Is Real and Consequential

**[SUPPORTED]**

The research reveals that meta-skills serve two simultaneous but structurally different roles in curriculum, and the doctrine treats them as one.

**Metaskills as curriculum goals** means: "Students should develop Exploring capacity through this program." This requires explicit naming of the metaskill in the curriculum, formative assessment of learner development, structured learner self-reflection, and deliberate transfer practice. The Skills Development Scotland framework, IB Approaches to Learning, and META-COIN project all operate primarily at this level. They define what meta-skills are, create progression frameworks for what they look like at different developmental stages, and build tools for learners to reflect on their own development.

**Metaskills as curriculum design principles** means: "This activity is structured using Exploring as an organizing principle." This is a different operation entirely. It means the *activity architecture* embodies the metaskill — an inquiry-driven activity when Exploring is targeted, a perspective-shifting exercise when Adapting is the focus, an empathy-centered protocol when Feeling is the design principle. Harvard's Visible Thinking Routines and Stanford's design thinking curriculum operate primarily at this level. They don't just name "critical thinking" as a goal — they provide specific, repeatable activity structures that *enact* critical thinking as a process.

The curriculum builder must encode both roles. A design constraint operates on the generation logic: it determines what *kind* of activity the tool produces when a given metaskill is targeted. A learning outcome operates on the validation logic: it checks whether the output includes explicit metaskill naming, learner reflection prompts, and assessment touchpoints. These are implemented differently and validated separately. [Source: metaskills-operationalization-evidence.md, Section 5]

## Finding 2: Tagging Is Necessary but Insufficient — The IB Implementation Gap

**[COMPLICATED]**

The IB Approaches to Learning framework provides the strongest cautionary evidence for how meta-skills operationalization can fail even with excellent design. The IB MYP requires schools to create ATL planning charts, embed ATL skills into unit planners, and provide structured student self-assessment. On paper, this looks like comprehensive operationalization. In practice, the Claremont implementation study found significant gaps: teachers embedded ATLs in planning documents but did not consistently make them visible in actual classroom activities. Students reported that teachers mentioned ATL skills but rarely connected them to specific activities they were doing. Over time, the implementation got *worse*, not better — fewer instances of explicit ATL embedding in classes despite continued planning-level compliance. [Source: metaskills-operationalization-evidence.md, Section 1.2]

The implication for the curriculum builder is direct: a tool that maps modules to metaskill tags — "this module develops Exploring" — without specifying *which activity within the module activates Exploring and through what pedagogical move* will reproduce the IB's documented implementation gap. The mapping layer is necessary for coverage validation, but the operationalization happens at the activity level, not the module level. The tool must generate activities that *enact* the targeted metaskill, not just label modules that *contain* it.

## Finding 3: Visible Thinking Routines Are the Operationalization Model

**[SUPPORTED]**

Harvard Project Zero's Visible Thinking Routines provide the strongest existing model for how to operationalize abstract cognitive capacities as concrete, repeatable classroom activities. The routines work because they are designed as cultural practices — short, structured sequences of steps or questions that get used repeatedly until they become part of how students think, not just what they do in one lesson. Each routine targets specific "thinking moves" (observation, interpretation, reasoning, perspective-taking, connection-making) and has three conditions for success: students must know how to apply a type of thinking, be motivated to think that way, and be sensitive to when that type of thinking is appropriate.

The key insight for the curriculum builder is that the operationalization unit is the *thinking routine* — a structured activity with defined prompts — not a curriculum label. The doctrine's six metaskills can each be operationalized through specific routine types. Exploring maps to inquiry routines (See-Think-Wonder). Adapting maps to belief-revision routines (I Used to Think... Now I Think...). Feeling maps to perspective-taking routines (Circle of Viewpoints). Creating maps to generation routines (brainstorming with constraints). This gives the curriculum builder a concrete activity vocabulary to draw from when generating activities that activate specific metaskills. [Source: metaskills-operationalization-evidence.md, Section 1.3]

## Finding 4: The Six Metaskills Fall on a Spectrum of Curriculum-Developability

**[COMPLICATED]**

Not all six metaskills are equally amenable to development through structured curriculum. The research converges on a clear hierarchy. Exploring (metacognition) has the strongest evidence base for direct curricular development, with Hattie reporting d = 0.69 and a 48-study meta-analysis finding g = 0.50 rising to g = 0.63 at follow-up. Creating (creativity) is directly teachable through deliberate practice with adaptive guidance, supported by a 70-study meta-analysis showing trained participants outperform controls 70% of the time. Feeling (emotional intelligence) is trainable as an ability under the Mayer-Salovey model, though debate exists about ability-EI versus trait-EI.

The picture becomes more complicated for the remaining three. Adapting (cognitive flexibility) is trainable, but a PMC review finds that "far transfer and longitudinal maintenance are rarely seen." More troublingly, one study found that "experimental training that capitalized on flexibility, novelty, and adaptiveness as central features did not lead to more progress than trainings without these elements" — suggesting the mechanism may be general practice and engagement rather than targeted flexibility training. Innovating (applied creativity under real constraints) is less studied than pure divergent thinking; the evidence base for teaching constrained innovation is thinner than for open-ended creativity. Imagining remains the weakest — the GAP flagged in Phase 2 is unresolved. No consolidated instructional evidence base exists for teaching imagination as a trainable cognitive capacity. [Source: metaskills-operationalization-evidence.md, Section 2.1]

This hierarchy has a direct design implication: the curriculum builder should not treat all six metaskills as equally achievable through a single curriculum. Earlier modules can focus on the high-developability metaskills (Exploring, Creating) where structured curriculum activities have strong evidence. Later modules can engage the harder-to-develop ones (Innovating under constraints, Adapting across domains) where the evidence supports practice-based development but not direct instruction. Imagining should be activated through adjacent evidence-based practices — scenario planning, futures thinking, mental simulation — rather than treated as a directly instructable capacity.

## Finding 5: Transfer Is the Critical Mechanism and Must Be Explicitly Designed

**[SUPPORTED]**

Multiple evidence streams converge on transfer as the central challenge in meta-skill development. The Harvard adaptive expertise research identifies the core tension: "when skills are taught in context, learners emerge with deeper, more nuanced understanding; however, skills are often embedded in the context in which they were learned and are, therefore, less transferable." The resolution proposed across Harvard PZ, McTighe's transfer research, and the OECD meta-learning framework is consistent: teach the skill in context AND include explicit transfer prompts that help learners "dis-embed" the skill and recognize when to apply it in other domains.

Transfer requires three metacognitive operations: planning (selecting and adapting strategies for new challenges), monitoring (identifying when a strategy is useful in a new context), and evaluating (determining how well it worked and why). This maps directly to Flavell's metacognition model and Zimmerman's self-regulated learning cycle — both well-validated frameworks. [Source: metaskills-operationalization-evidence.md, Section 2.2]

For the curriculum builder, this means every activity that targets a metaskill must include an explicit transfer prompt — a structured reflection question that asks: "Where else could you apply this thinking? When have you seen this pattern before? How would this work in a different context?" Without this, the curriculum develops domain-specific competence but not transferable meta-capacity. The SDS framework models this with its learner self-evaluation tools; the Visible Thinking Routine "Connect-Extend-Challenge" operationalizes it directly.

## Finding 6: Design Thinking Provides Activity Archetypes for Five of Six Metaskills

**[SUPPORTED]**

Design thinking curricula (Stanford d.school, IDEO, HBS) provide concrete activity archetypes that operationalize five of the doctrine's six metaskills through a specific process structure. The Empathize phase activates Feeling through user interviews, observation protocols, and empathy maps. Define activates Exploring through problem reframing and root cause analysis. Ideate activates Creating and Imagining through constrained brainstorming and combinatorial thinking. Prototype activates Innovating through rapid building with real constraints. Test activates Adapting through iterative feedback and belief revision based on evidence.

A University of Texas analysis identifies a "pedagogy problem" in design thinking education: accelerated models create tension between speed and genuine skill development. Self-regulated learning interventions must be "strategically embedded" to compensate. A Stanford study found that design-thinking curriculum had its strongest effects on lower-achieving students, suggesting the structured activities may be most valuable precisely for learners who have not yet developed these capacities independently. [Source: metaskills-operationalization-evidence.md, Section 3.1]

The curriculum builder can draw on design thinking's activity vocabulary when generating metaskill-activating exercises — but should not wholesale adopt the design thinking process as the curriculum's structural framework. Design thinking is one source of activity archetypes, not the organizing logic. The organizing logic remains the doctrine's TMA at the content level, wrapped in the session design framework from Phase 6.

## Finding 7: What the Metaskill-Mapping Layer Should Contain

**[EMERGING — synthesized from multiple frameworks, not empirically validated as a unit]**

Convergent evidence from SDS progression frameworks, IB ATL planning charts, health professions competency mapping, and AI-powered curriculum mapping tools points to a specific structure for the metaskill-mapping layer:

**Per module, the tool should generate and validate:**
1. **Primary metaskill(s):** Which of the six are the deliberate focus
2. **Activation activity:** The specific activity or thinking routine that activates each targeted metaskill — not a label but a described pedagogical move with prompts
3. **Observable indicator:** What behavior or output demonstrates that the metaskill is being exercised (from SDS progression framework model)
4. **Transfer prompt:** An explicit reflection question that helps learners dis-embed the metaskill from this specific context
5. **Assessment touchpoint:** How metaskill development is formatively assessed

**Across the program, the tool should validate:**
6. **Coverage:** Each of the six metaskills must be deliberately activated in a minimum number of modules
7. **Distribution:** No single metaskill should dominate the program; balanced activation across all six
8. **Progression:** Earlier modules focus on high-developability metaskills (Exploring, Creating); later modules engage harder-to-develop ones (Innovating, Adapting in novel domains)

This is the most prescriptive finding in this phase, and it should be treated as a design hypothesis to be tested in Phase 10 (Tool Architecture) rather than a validated specification. The individual components are each supported by evidence, but the integrated schema has not been tested as a unit.

## Finding 8: The "Full Range" Problem Is Now an Operationalization Problem, Not Just a Taxonomy Problem

**[COMPLICATED]**

Phase 2 flagged that the doctrine's "full range of human cognitive and emotional capacity" claim is contradicted by every major comparative framework — all include social/interpersonal dimensions (communication, collaboration, leadership) absent from the doctrine's six. Phase 7 makes this gap more consequential: when you operationalize metaskills, you need to specify concrete activities for each one. The SDS framework operationalizes 12 sub-skills including Communicating, Collaborating, and Leading. The IB ATL operationalizes Communication Skills and Social Skills as distinct categories with their own activity types. If the curriculum builder only operationalizes the doctrine's six, it will systematically omit activity types — collaborative problem-solving, structured debate, peer teaching, team-based design challenges — that every major framework considers essential for meta-skill development.

The doctrine has two defensible options, both of which require explicit articulation:
1. **Expand:** Add a 7th metaskill (e.g., "Connecting" or "Collaborating") that covers the social/interpersonal dimension
2. **Reframe:** Argue that the six are *individual* capacities and that social skills emerge as their applied expressions (Feeling expressed socially becomes collaboration; Exploring expressed socially becomes collective inquiry) — but then operationalize activities that specifically activate those social expressions

Either way, the curriculum builder needs collaborative activity types in its generation vocabulary. The question is whether they are tagged as activating a named metaskill or treated as a delivery mode for the existing six.

## Implications for the Curriculum Builder

### What the tool must do:
1. **Generate metaskill-activating activities, not just metaskill labels.** The operationalization unit is the activity (thinking routine, design exercise, reflection protocol), not the module tag.
2. **Include transfer prompts in every metaskill-targeted activity.** Without explicit dis-embedding, metaskills develop as context-embedded competence rather than transferable capacity.
3. **Validate metaskill coverage across the program.** Mapping layer checks that all six are activated, distributed, and progressively developed.
4. **Distinguish between design-constraint and learning-outcome roles.** The metaskill shapes what kind of activity is generated (design constraint) AND appears explicitly in the curriculum as something learners reflect on (learning outcome).
5. **Respect the developability hierarchy.** Don't promise equal development of all six through curriculum alone. Exploring and Creating have strong evidence; Imagining has a gap.

### What the tool should not do:
1. **Treat metaskill tagging as operationalization.** Labeling a module "develops Exploring" without specifying the activating activity is the IB ATL failure mode.
2. **Treat all six as equally curriculum-developable.** The evidence hierarchy is real.
3. **Omit collaborative activity types.** Whether or not the taxonomy is expanded, the activity vocabulary must include social/interpersonal formats.
4. **Rely on implicit development.** Phase 2 meta-analysis: metacognitive development requires explicit prompts (d = 0.48); implicit structural exposure does not produce meta-skill gains.
