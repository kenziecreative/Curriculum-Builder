# Session Design Templates from Instructional Design Practice

**Research Question**: Are there established session design templates from instructional design practice (e.g., Gagne's Nine Events, Merrill's First Principles applied at session level) that a curriculum builder tool should draw on?

**Date**: 2026-03-15
**Finding Tag**: SUPPORTED — Multiple well-established, evidence-backed session design frameworks exist with clear structural templates that can be operationalized in a curriculum builder tool.

---

## 1. Gagne's Nine Events of Instruction

**Source**: Robert Gagne, *The Conditions of Learning* (1965; revised editions through 1992)
**Level of application**: Individual lesson/session
**Cognitive basis**: Information processing model of learning

### The Nine Events (in sequence)

1. **Gain Attention** — Capture learner focus through stimuli (provocative question, multimedia, surprising fact)
2. **Inform Learners of Objectives** — State what learners will be able to do after the session
3. **Stimulate Recall of Prior Learning** — Activate existing knowledge to create scaffolding for new content
4. **Present the Content** — Deliver new material in logical, organized manner (lectures, demonstrations, readings)
5. **Provide Learning Guidance** — Offer explanations, examples, and demonstrations to support encoding into long-term memory
6. **Elicit Performance** — Require active demonstration or practice of new knowledge
7. **Provide Feedback** — Give specific information about accuracy and quality of learner responses
8. **Assess Performance** — Formally evaluate mastery of objectives (quizzes, projects, demonstrations)
9. **Enhance Retention and Transfer** — Promote application to real-world contexts and new situations

### Research Evidence
- No single definitive study proving effectiveness, but framework is "clearly aligned with established learning principles" (multiple university instructional design centers)
- PMC study (2015) on undergraduate nursing courses using Gagne's 9 Events: "Student evaluation ratings indicated enhanced teacher mastery, effectiveness, and enthusiasm. Overall student final grades increased."
- PMC study (2014) validated Gagne's model for teaching psychomotor skills
- PMC study (2022) applied Gagne's framework to microlearning contexts

### Implications for Curriculum Builder
- Most prescriptive session-level template available — each event maps to a concrete design decision
- Events are presented as linear but practitioners move fluidly between them; tool should allow reordering
- Strongest fit for structured knowledge/skill transfer sessions
- Each event can be turned into a template field or prompt in a builder tool

## 2. Merrill's First Principles of Instruction

**Source**: M. David Merrill, "First Principles of Instruction," *Educational Technology Research and Development*, 50(3), 43-59 (2002)
**Level of application**: Session or module (flexible)
**Cognitive basis**: Synthesis across multiple instructional design theories

### The Five Principles (Task-Centered Cycle)

1. **Task/Problem-Centered** — Learning begins with a real-world task or problem, not abstract content
2. **Activation** — Learners connect new information to existing knowledge and past experiences
3. **Demonstration** — Instructor shows how to solve the problem using worked examples
4. **Application** — Learners practice the new skill with coaching and feedback
5. **Integration** — Learners adapt and apply the skill to their actual professional/life context

### Research Evidence
- Merrill derived these principles by reviewing multiple instructional design theories to identify prescriptive principles common across them — a meta-theoretical synthesis rather than a single experiment
- The principles do NOT prescribe a fixed order; they can be adapted to context
- Can be embedded in almost any instructional format

### Implications for Curriculum Builder
- More flexible than Gagne — works as a checklist rather than a sequence
- Problem-centered framing is especially relevant for professional/adult learning
- A builder tool could use Merrill's principles as validation criteria: "Does this session include activation? Demonstration? Application? Integration?"
- Strong alignment with the project's Theory-Method-Application framework

## 3. Rosenshine's 10 Principles of Instruction

**Source**: Barak Rosenshine, "Principles of Instruction: Research-Based Strategies That All Teachers Should Know," *American Educator*, Spring 2012 (ERIC ID: EJ971753)
**Level of application**: Individual lesson
**Cognitive basis**: Three converging research streams — cognitive science, master teacher studies, cognitive supports

### The 10 Principles

1. Begin a lesson with a short review of previous learning
2. Present new material in small steps with student practice after each step
3. Ask a large number of questions and check responses of all students
4. Provide models of worked-out problems
5. Guide student practice (think aloud, model steps)
6. Check for student understanding
7. Obtain a high success rate
8. Provide scaffolds for difficult tasks
9. Require and monitor independent practice
10. Engage students in weekly and monthly review

### Organization into Four Strands (Tom Sherrington's synthesis)
- **Sequencing concepts and modelling**
- **Questioning**
- **Reviewing material**
- **Stages of practice**

### Research Evidence
- Derived from research on "master teachers" — teachers whose students consistently made the highest gains
- Rosenshine explicitly warned against converting principles into rigid lesson formulas or compliance checklists
- "When the principles are translated into mandatory lesson structures, observed through compliance checklists, or reduced to a prescribed sequence regardless of content or cohort, the flexibility that makes the principles effective is lost"

### Implications for Curriculum Builder
- Best used as quality criteria rather than a rigid template
- The warning about over-prescriptive application is directly relevant to building an automated tool — a builder should suggest these elements, not enforce a rigid checklist
- Sherrington's four-strand grouping provides a practical organizational scheme

## 4. Madeline Hunter's ITIP (Instructional Theory into Practice)

**Source**: Madeline Hunter, UCLA (developed 1960s-1980s)
**Level of application**: Individual lesson
**Also known as**: Seven-Step Lesson Plan, Hunter Model

### The Seven Components

1. **Anticipatory Set** — Prepare students by piquing interest, creating mental images, focusing attention
2. **State the Objective** — Communicate what learners will learn and why it matters
3. **Instructional Input** — Provide information needed to achieve the objective
4. **Modeling** — Demonstrate how to achieve the objective with examples
5. **Check for Understanding** — Verify students followed the instruction
6. **Guided Practice** — Students apply content with instructor monitoring and feedback
7. **Independent Practice** — Students practice autonomously (homework/projects)

### Important Historical Note
Hunter herself did NOT create a rigid seven-step lesson plan model. She suggested various instructional elements that were subsequently compiled by others into the "Seven Step Lesson Plan" and used as a checklist. This is a cautionary tale for curriculum builder tools about the risk of over-formalizing flexible frameworks.

### Research Evidence
- Gained widespread adoption in American schools during 1980s-1990s
- Mixed research results on effectiveness compared to other approaches
- Effectiveness depends heavily on quality of implementation and specific learning outcomes

### Implications for Curriculum Builder
- Historical precedent shows that converting flexible teaching guidance into rigid templates can backfire
- The "Gradual Release of Responsibility" pattern (I Do / We Do / You Do) derived from this model has stronger research support than the full Hunter model itself
- Direct ancestor of much current session design thinking

## 5. The 5E Instructional Model

**Source**: Rodger W. Bybee and team at BSCS (Biological Sciences Curriculum Study), 1987
**Based on**: Atkin-Karplus Learning Cycle; SCIS Learning Cycle (Karplus & Thier, 1962-1977)
**Level of application**: Individual lesson or short unit
**Primary domain**: Science education (but adapted broadly)

### The Five Phases

1. **Engage** — Focus attention on phenomenon/problem; activate prior knowledge; draw connections
2. **Explore** — Teacher-guided inquiry; students solve cognitive conflict; build explanations
3. **Explain** — Students make sense of data; develop vocabulary; defend rationale
4. **Elaborate** — Deepen understanding by applying to new/related situations; address misconceptions
5. **Evaluate** — Integrated throughout (not just at end); assess understanding at each phase

### Extensions
- **7E Model** (Eisenkraft, 2003): Added "Elicit" (before Engage) and "Extend" (after Elaborate) to make prior-conception activation and far transfer more explicit

### Research Evidence — Meta-Analytic Data
- **Polanin, Austin, Taylor, Steingut, Rodgers, & Williams (2024)**: Systematic review and meta-analysis published in *AERA Open* — "Overall meta-analytic results demonstrated a positive significant effect on all outcomes"
- **5E and 7E vs. 3E models**: 7E model (g = 1.23) had "statistically significantly larger effects" than 3E model (g = 0.42)
- **5E vs. 7E directly**: Some studies found no significant difference; others found 7E slightly favored, moderated by fidelity of implementation and teacher training
- Multiple studies show "significant improvements in conceptual learning, development of scientific skills, and interest in science"

### Implications for Curriculum Builder
- Strong meta-analytic evidence base (strongest quantitative evidence of any framework reviewed)
- Inquiry-oriented structure — best fit for exploration/discovery sessions
- The 7E extension addresses exactly the gaps (prior knowledge elicitation, transfer) that other frameworks also emphasize
- Evaluation-throughout-not-just-at-end is a design principle the builder should enforce

## 6. Understanding by Design (UbD) / Backward Design

**Source**: Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe, *Understanding by Design* (1998; 2005 expanded edition)
**Level of application**: Unit and lesson level
**Approach**: Design backward from desired outcomes

### The Three Stages

1. **Stage 1: Identify Desired Results** — Clarify learning priorities, long-term outcomes, and short-term goals
2. **Stage 2: Determine Assessment Evidence** — "Think like assessors" before planning activities; design assessments that show extent of learning
3. **Stage 3: Plan Learning Experiences and Instruction** — Design activities and instruction to support the outcomes identified in Stage 1

### Template
Wiggins and McTighe developed a formal unit planning Template — a graphic organizer depicting the 3 stages with planning questions for each. A 2-page version includes guiding questions per stage.

### Implications for Curriculum Builder
- UbD is a planning methodology more than a session structure — it tells you HOW to plan, not what a session looks like minute-by-minute
- Strong complement to session-level frameworks like Gagne or 5E: use UbD to determine what the session should achieve, then use a session template to structure the delivery
- The "backward" principle (start with outcomes, then assessment, then activities) should be embedded in any curriculum builder workflow

## 7. ADDIE Model

**Source**: Originally developed for U.S. military training (Florida State University, 1975); widely attributed and adapted
**Level of application**: Course/program level (macro), but adapted to session level

### The Five Phases

1. **Analyze** — Identify learner needs, learning context, constraints
2. **Design** — Craft overall structure, objectives, assessment strategy
3. **Develop** — Create materials, lesson plans, interactive content
4. **Implement** — Deliver instruction
5. **Evaluate** — Assess effectiveness, iterate

### Session-Level Adaptation (from SessionLab and others)
Common session template headings: Overview, Objectives, In Preparation, Class and Individual Activities, Reflection, Enrichment Activities/Resources, References

### Implications for Curriculum Builder
- ADDIE is a process model, not a session template — it describes the workflow of creating instruction
- A curriculum builder tool IS an ADDIE implementation: it should guide users through analysis, design, development, implementation planning, and evaluation design
- Session-level templates from other frameworks (Gagne, 5E, etc.) would be used WITHIN the ADDIE Design and Develop phases

## 8. Dick and Carey Systems Approach

**Source**: Walter Dick and Lou Carey, *The Systematic Design of Instruction* (1978; multiple editions)
**Level of application**: Course/program level with session-level implications

### Key Contribution
Views instruction as a system where "instructor, learners, materials, instructional activities, delivery system, and learning work together to produce desired outcomes." Emphasizes iterative design with formative and summative evaluation.

### Implications for Curriculum Builder
- Reinforces systems thinking — a builder should not generate sessions in isolation but consider how they connect
- Learner analysis and context analysis as prerequisites to session design

## 9. Explicit Direct Instruction (EDI)

**Source**: John Hollingsworth and Silvia Ybarra, DataWorks Educational Research
**Level of application**: Individual lesson
**Cognitive basis**: Cognitive Load Theory, Rosenshine's research

### Research Evidence
- **Hattie's meta-analyses**: Direct instruction effect size = 0.59 (above the 0.40 threshold for "one year's growth in one year's time")
- EDI "activates 18 of the top 30 effects (out of 195 total)" in Hattie's Visible Learning framework
- "Explicit instruction reduces cognitive load" and "the extent to which instruction is made explicit directly impacts both student achievement and independent, self-regulated learning"
- "Effective instruction requires both well-designed lessons and well-taught lessons — you can't compensate for poor design with great delivery"

### Implications for Curriculum Builder
- The finding that you cannot compensate for poor design with great delivery is the core argument FOR a curriculum builder tool that enforces good session design
- Effect size of 0.59 for direct instruction provides quantitative justification for structured session templates

## 10. WIPPEA Model (Adult Learning)

**Source**: U.S. Department of Education, TEAL Center
**Level of application**: Individual session (adult/professional education)

### The Six Phases

1. **Warm-up** — Make learners comfortable; help them realize what they already know
2. **Introduction** — State objectives and relevance
3. **Presentation** — Deliver new content
4. **Practice** — Apply content with support
5. **Evaluation** — Assess understanding
6. **Application** — Connect to real-world use

### Implications for Curriculum Builder
- Specifically designed for adult learners — directly relevant to professional development curricula
- Warm-up phase distinct from engagement — addresses adult motivational needs
- Application as final phase (not just practice) aligns with transfer-focused design

## Cross-Framework Synthesis: Common Session Design Elements

Across all frameworks reviewed, the following elements appear consistently:

| Session Element | Gagne | Merrill | Rosenshine | Hunter | 5E | WIPPEA |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Activate prior knowledge | Event 3 | Activation | Principle 1 | Anticipatory Set | Engage | Warm-up |
| State objectives | Event 2 | (implicit) | — | State Objective | — | Introduction |
| Present new content | Event 4 | Demonstration | Principle 2 | Instructional Input | Explain | Presentation |
| Model/demonstrate | Event 5 | Demonstration | Principles 4-5 | Modeling | Explore/Explain | Presentation |
| Guided practice | Event 6 | Application | Principles 5-6 | Guided Practice | Elaborate | Practice |
| Check understanding | Event 7 | (feedback loop) | Principles 3,6 | Check Understanding | Evaluate | Evaluation |
| Independent practice | Event 6 | Application | Principle 9 | Independent Practice | Elaborate | Practice |
| Assess mastery | Event 8 | (implicit) | Principle 7 | — | Evaluate | Evaluation |
| Promote transfer | Event 9 | Integration | Principle 10 | — | Elaborate/Evaluate | Application |
| Review/spaced practice | Event 1 | — | Principles 1,10 | — | — | Warm-up |

### The Universal Session Arc

Distilling across all frameworks, a minimal evidence-based session structure contains:

1. **Open** — Activate prior knowledge + state purpose (Gagne 1-3, Merrill Activation, Rosenshine 1)
2. **Present** — Introduce new material in manageable chunks with demonstrations (Gagne 4-5, Merrill Demonstration, Rosenshine 2,4)
3. **Practice** — Guided then independent application with feedback (Gagne 6-7, Merrill Application, Rosenshine 5-9)
4. **Close** — Assess, consolidate, and connect to transfer (Gagne 8-9, Merrill Integration, Rosenshine 10)

## Key Data Points for Curriculum Builder Design

1. **Effect sizes**: Direct/explicit instruction d = 0.59 (Hattie); 5E/7E models g = 0.42 to 1.23 depending on variant (Polanin et al., 2024)
2. **Over-prescription warning**: Both Rosenshine and Hunter's legacy show that converting flexible frameworks into rigid checklists reduces effectiveness
3. **Backward design principle**: UbD's insight that session design should start from outcomes, not activities, should be the builder's entry point
4. **"Poor design cannot be compensated by great delivery"** — This is the foundational justification for a design tool
5. **Adult learning distinction**: WIPPEA and Merrill's models emphasize real-world problems and integration — more appropriate for professional development than models designed primarily for K-12
6. **No single best model**: Different session types (skill acquisition vs. conceptual exploration vs. problem-solving) call for different frameworks. A builder should offer multiple templates, not one.

---

## Recommendations for the Curriculum Builder Tool

1. **Implement the Universal Session Arc** as the default template (Open-Present-Practice-Close), with the option to expand into any specific framework
2. **Offer framework-specific templates** for different learning goals:
   - Gagne's 9 Events for structured skill/knowledge transfer
   - 5E/7E for inquiry and exploration sessions
   - Merrill's First Principles for problem-centered professional development
3. **Use Merrill's principles as validation criteria** — check every generated session for activation, demonstration, application, and integration
4. **Enforce backward design** — require learning objectives and assessment evidence before generating activities
5. **Build in flexibility warnings** — when templates are applied too rigidly, surface Rosenshine's caution about over-prescription
6. **Distinguish between process models (ADDIE, Dick & Carey) and session templates (Gagne, 5E)** — the builder itself IS the process model; it generates session templates
