# DCR Learning Model — Evidence Base Assessment
## Deconstruct → Draw Comparisons → Rebuild

**Research Note | March 2026**
**Purpose:** Assess the learning science foundations for the DCR model as described in the curriculum builder doctrine.

---

## Overview of Claims Under Investigation

The doctrine asserts:
1. Experts in wicked domains pattern-match, pull from analogous experiences, deconstruct problems into familiar components, and rebuild working models fast.
2. This process can be made explicit and taught.
3. Deconstruct maps to the "Exploring" metaskill; Rebuild maps to "Adapting."
4. The model is designed for wicked environments where the 10,000-hour rule breaks down.
5. DCR is a "practiced orientation" that fast learners do reflexively.

---

## 1. Analogical Reasoning and Transfer — The "Draw Comparisons" Move

### Gentner's Structure-Mapping Theory (1983)

**Core claim:** An analogy is a mapping of knowledge from a base domain into a target domain that preserves relational structure. The key mechanism is *systematicity*: mappings are governed by higher-order relations, not surface attribute similarity. Analogical reasoning involves (a) recognizing structural similarity, (b) mapping relations from base to target, and (c) generating inferences.

- Gentner, D. (1983). Structure-mapping: A theoretical framework for analogy. *Cognitive Science, 7*(2), 155–170.
- Computational formalization: the Structure-Mapping Engine (SME) demonstrated the theory's tractability.

**Status: SUPPORTED** — Structure-mapping theory gives DCR's "Draw Comparisons" move a rigorous cognitive mechanism. The theory explains *why* comparison works: it forces abstraction of relational structure away from surface features, which is exactly what enables transfer to novel situations.

**Complication:** Spontaneous analogical transfer is unreliable. Experts retrieve structurally similar analogs; novices retrieve surface-similar ones. This creates a paradox: the people who most need to draw comparisons (novices) are least likely to do it well without scaffolding. Memory retrieval depends heavily on surface similarity, even when accuracy depends on structural matching.

**Status: COMPLICATED** — The DCR doctrine assumes learners can reliably "draw comparisons" across domains. The evidence shows this is skill-dependent and unreliable without explicit structure.

---

### Gentner, Loewenstein & Thompson (2003) — Analogical Encoding

**Core claim:** Learning by comparing two cases simultaneously (analogical encoding) is more effective than studying cases sequentially. Three studies on negotiation learning showed:
- Experiment 1: Comparing two cases vs. no case study showed significant benefit.
- Experiment 2: Comparing two cases simultaneously showed a *marked advantage* over studying the same two cases separately.
- Experiment 3: Increasing comparison support directly increased transfer rates in live negotiation exercises.

Mechanism: "Comparison processes can reveal common structure and combine partial structures and thus promote transfer, even early in learning when neither example is fully understood. Comparison invites an alignment and re-representation of examples, yielding a common representation that is less context-specific than the initial ones."

- Gentner, D., Loewenstein, J., & Thompson, L. (2003). Learning and transfer: A general role for analogical encoding. *Journal of Educational Psychology, 95*, 393–408.

**Status: STRONGLY SUPPORTED** — This is the most direct empirical support for the "Draw Comparisons" move in DCR. The active process of comparison — not just exposure to multiple examples — is what drives schema abstraction and transfer. The negotiation context makes this directly relevant to professional learning in complex domains.

---

### Alfieri, Nokes-Malach & Schunn (2013) — Meta-Analysis on Case Comparisons

**Core claim:** Analogical comparison (comparing cases) leads to better learning outcomes than instruction based on sequential, single, or non-analogous cases. Effect size: **d = 0.50** across lab and classroom settings.

- Alfieri, L., Nokes-Malach, T. J., & Schunn, C. D. (2013). Learning through case comparisons: A meta-analytic review. *Educational Psychologist, 48*(2), 87–113.

**Status: STRONGLY SUPPORTED** — A medium effect size (d = 0.50) across multiple contexts is substantial for a cognitive intervention. This meta-analysis establishes comparison-based learning as a robust instructional technique, not just a theoretical claim.

**Important nuance:** The effect is strongest when the compared cases share the same *underlying structure* but differ on surface features. Comparing poorly chosen cases (too similar, too dissimilar, or structurally unrelated) can fail or backfire. Instructional design of comparison pairs matters enormously.

## 2. Wicked vs. Kind Learning Environments

### Hogarth, Lejarraga & Soyer (2015) — The Two Settings

**Core claim:** Expertise requires two aligned settings: the learning environment (where cues and feedback are acquired) and the application environment (where inferences are used). Kind environments involve close matches between these settings; wicked environments involve mismatches.

- Kind environments: Stable rules, repetitive patterns, quick accurate feedback. Example: chess, weather forecasting.
- Wicked environments: Rules change, feedback is absent/delayed/inaccurate, causal links blurred. Example: most professional domains — management, medicine, law, strategy.

Key implication: In wicked environments, experience does not necessarily produce expertise — it can produce *confident miscalibration*. Feedback loops that are noisy or delayed undermine the signal extraction that expertise normally depends on.

- Hogarth, R. M., Lejarraga, T., & Soyer, E. (2015). The two settings of kind and wicked learning environments. *Current Directions in Psychological Science, 24*(5), 379–385.

**Status: STRONGLY SUPPORTED** — This validates one of DCR's core premises: standard expertise development (the kind that produces the 10,000-hour effect) fails in wicked environments. In wicked domains, you can practice for years and become confidently wrong.

**Extension by Kahneman & Klein (2009):** Their adversarial collaboration concluded that intuitive expertise is only valid when: (a) the environment is regular enough that cues are valid signals, and (b) the learner has had sufficient practice to learn those regularities. Both conditions fail in many professional domains. — Kahneman, D., & Klein, G. (2009). Conditions for intuitive expertise: A failure to disagree. *American Psychologist, 64*(6), 515–526.

---

### Epstein's "Range" (2019) — Wicked Domains and Breadth

**Core claim:** In wicked domains, early specialization is less effective than a "sampling period" of diverse experience. Breadth of training predicts breadth of transfer. Diverse experience leads to abstract models that are less anchored to specific examples — enabling creative application to novel situations.

- Epstein, D. (2019). *Range: Why Generalists Triumph in a Specialized World.* Riverhead Books.

Evidence cited: Nobel laureates and leading scientists show broader experience profiles than average domain peers. Technological inventors who accumulated experience in multiple domains had greater creative impact than deep specialists.

**Status: SUPPORTED (with caveats)** — The evidence for the value of breadth in wicked domains is real but comes largely from observational/retrospective data, not controlled experiments. The causal mechanism (breadth → abstract models → transfer) is plausible and consistent with analogical encoding research, but harder to establish than Epstein's narrative suggests.

**Complication for DCR:** The DCR model assumes learners already have cross-domain experience to draw comparisons from. For learners at the start of their careers with narrow experience bases, the comparison step may be underpowered. DCR may work best when learners have enough breadth to have meaningful analogs — which is itself a developmental condition.

---

### Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald (2014) — Deliberate Practice Meta-Analysis

**Core claim:** Deliberate practice is important but explains far less variance in performance than Ericsson's original model claimed. Variance explained by deliberate practice by domain:
- Games: 26%
- Music: 21%
- Sports: 18%
- Education: 4%
- Professions: <1%

The finding for professions (the wicked domain par excellence) is stark: deliberate practice explains almost nothing.

- Macnamara, B. N., Hambrick, D. Z., & Oswald, F. L. (2014). Deliberate practice and performance in music, games, sports, education, and professions: A meta-analysis. *Psychological Science, 25*(8), 1608–1618.

**Status: SUPPORTED** — This validates DCR's implicit critique of the 10,000-hour framing for professional domains. At elite levels, deliberate practice within a single domain accounts for almost none of the variance in professional performance. Other factors — cognitive abilities, breadth of exposure, analogical capacity, adaptive strategy — must account for the difference.

**Debate:** Ericsson (2016) disputed the methodology, arguing that Macnamara's definition of deliberate practice was too broad. Macnamara et al. (2016) rebutted, identifying contradictions and omissions in Ericsson's critique. The consensus reading: deliberate practice matters for kind domains with clear feedback and defined skill paths, but has far weaker effects in complex professional environments.

## 3. Expert Problem-Solving — Does the Doctrine Match Cognitive Science?

### Chi, Feltovich & Glaser (1981) — Expert-Novice Categorization

**Core finding:** Experts and novices categorize the same physics problems differently. Novices use *surface features* (inclined planes, pulleys, springs). Experts use *deep structure* (conservation of energy, Newton's Second Law). Four experiments confirmed:
- Experts abstract principles first, then solve.
- Novices represent problems literally, then try to match procedures.

- Chi, M. T. H., Feltovich, P. J., & Glaser, R. (1981). Categorization and representation of physics problems by experts and novices. *Cognitive Science, 5*(2), 121–152.

**Status: STRONGLY SUPPORTED** — This directly validates the "Deconstruct" move in DCR. Experts don't look at a problem as a monolithic whole — they immediately decompose it into principled categories. The Deconstruct step is not a metaphor; it describes what expert cognition actually does. The critical implication: experts see *structure*, novices see *surface*. Teaching decomposition is teaching the experts' representational move.

---

### Chase & Simon (1973) — Chess Chunking Theory

**Core finding:** Expert chess players' superior recall of board positions depends on chunking: experts store 50,000–100,000 meaningful patterns (chunks) in long-term memory. Recognizing a chunk triggers associated strategic responses. The chunks represent compressed, structured knowledge — not raw memory for positions.

- Chase, W. G., & Simon, H. A. (1973). Perception in chess. *Cognitive Psychology, 4*(1), 55–81.

**Status: SUPPORTED** — This provides a mechanism for the pattern recognition that DCR's Deconstruct step relies on. But it also complicates the model: chunking is built through massive exposure in *kind* environments (chess has stable rules and immediate feedback). The mechanism transfers poorly to wicked domains where valid patterns are harder to identify and verify.

---

### Klein's Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) Model (1993, 1999)

**Core finding:** Experienced decision-makers (firefighters, emergency medical technicians, nuclear technicians) rarely compare options. Instead, they recognize situations as familiar, generate a first plausible course of action, and mentally simulate its execution. This recognition-before-deliberation process is fast and effective in high-stress situations.

Mechanism: Cue recognition → situation assessment → mental simulation → action. Expert judgment is not comparative but *recognitional*.

- Klein, G. A. (1993). A recognition-primed decision (RPD) model of rapid decision making. In G. A. Klein, J. Orasanu, R. Calderwood, & C. Zsambok (Eds.), *Decision making in action: Models and methods* (pp. 138–147). Ablex.
- Klein, G. A. (1999). *Sources of power: How people make decisions.* MIT Press.

**Status: COMPLICATED** — Klein's RPD model describes expert performance in *relatively kind* environments (firefighting has harsh but consistent feedback). The RPD model actually argues *against* the primacy of explicit comparison — experts don't "Draw Comparisons" consciously; they recognize. This is a tension in DCR's architecture: the model may be describing a *learning* process more than an *in-the-moment* performance process. DCR may be better understood as how to *develop* expert recognitional capacity, not how experts make decisions under pressure.

## 4. Cognitive Flexibility Theory — Validating the "Rebuild" Move

### Spiro, Feltovich, Jacobson & Coulson (1992) — Cognitive Flexibility in Ill-Structured Domains

**Core claim:** Learning in ill-structured domains requires instruction that "criss-crosses" the knowledge landscape — revisiting the same material at different times, in rearranged contexts, for different purposes, and from different conceptual perspectives. This prevents the two pathological failure modes: oversimplification and rigid knowledge application.

Key principles:
1. Multiple representations of content are required (no single framing suffices).
2. Knowledge must be context-embedded, not context-free.
3. Instructional materials should be case-based, not principle-first.
4. Knowledge sources should be highly interconnected, not compartmentalized.

Empirical finding: Compared to traditional instruction, hypertext-based criss-crossing led to *lower* performance on factual memory measures but *higher* performance on transfer tasks. The tradeoff is real and intentional.

- Spiro, R. J., Feltovich, P. J., Jacobson, M. J., & Coulson, R. L. (1992). Cognitive flexibility, constructivism, and hypertext: Random access instruction for advanced knowledge acquisition in ill-structured domains. In T. Duffy & D. Jonassen (Eds.), *Constructivism and the technology of instruction* (pp. 57–75). Lawrence Erlbaum.

**Status: STRONGLY SUPPORTED** — Cognitive Flexibility Theory (CFT) is the most direct theoretical backing for DCR's Rebuild step. The "Rebuild" move — reconstructing a working model from multiple perspectives and analogous cases — is exactly what CFT recommends for advanced knowledge acquisition in complex domains. CFT also explains *why* simple instruction fails in wicked domains: it produces rigid, oversimplified representations that can't flex under novel conditions.

**Important constraint from CFT:** Criss-crossing works for *advanced* knowledge acquisition — learners who already have some foothold in a domain. For complete novices with no priors to compare against, the approach can be overwhelming. This suggests DCR is most powerful as a transition strategy (novice → intermediate → expert) rather than a pure beginner framework.

## 5. Teachability of the DCR Orientation

### Teaching Analogical Reasoning

**Evidence base:**
- Analogical reasoning is malleable and teachable through explicit strategy instruction (Gentner & Loewenstein, 2002).
- University students frequently *fail* to employ structural analogy spontaneously, though they can apply it effectively when given explicit comparison prompts.
- Experts in a domain retrieve structurally similar analogs more than novices — but novices who are prompted to compare cases close the gap significantly.
- A critical dissociation: memory retrieval depends on surface similarity; transfer accuracy depends on structural similarity. Explicit comparison instruction decouples these by forcing structural alignment before action.

- Gentner, D., & Loewenstein, J. (2002). Relational language and relational thought. In E. Amsel & J. Byrnes (Eds.), *Language, literacy, and cognitive development* (pp. 87–120). Lawrence Erlbaum.

**Status: SUPPORTED** — Analogical reasoning can be taught, but requires explicit scaffolding and deliberate comparison prompts. Passive exposure to multiple examples is insufficient; structured comparison is required. This validates DCR's teachability claim but implies specific instructional design requirements — the "Draw Comparisons" step must be actively scaffolded, not left to the learner.

---

### Teaching Metacognitive Strategies

**Evidence base:**
- Schraw & Dennison (1994) identified two components of metacognition: knowledge of cognition (declarative, procedural, conditional knowledge about strategy use) and regulation of cognition (planning, monitoring, evaluating).
- Schraw, G., & Dennison, R. S. (1994). Assessing metacognitive awareness. *Contemporary Educational Psychology, 19*(4), 460–475.
- Effective metacognitive instruction must address *conditional* knowledge — not just *how* to use a strategy but *when* it applies.
- Research shows metacognitive skills positively correlate with GPA and learning outcomes; explicit instruction improves metacognitive subprocess scores over a first year of college.

**Status: SUPPORTED** — Metacognition is teachable, and the gains generalize. The critical finding for DCR: the most effective strategy instruction teaches "when to use the strategy" — which is precisely the "practiced orientation" DCR describes. Teaching DCR as a meta-strategy (a strategy for approaching new problems) rather than a content strategy requires developing conditional metacognitive knowledge.

---

### The Far Transfer Problem

**Critical complication:** Far transfer — applying learning across substantially different contexts — is notoriously difficult to induce. A second-order meta-analysis of cognitive training found:
- Near transfer effect: g = 0.44 (significant).
- Far transfer effect: g = 0.11 (not convincing).

The difficulty of far transfer is considered by some researchers an "invariant of human cognition" — not just a teaching failure but a deep constraint on how knowledge organizes itself.

**Status: COMPLICATED** — If DCR depends on learners spontaneously applying comparison logic *across* domains (far transfer), the evidence suggests this is the hardest part of the whole model to produce. Learners can be taught to compare cases *within* a domain reasonably well; getting them to bridge domains reflexively requires sustained, varied practice with explicit structure that most curricula don't provide.

## 6. Mental Models and Schema Reconstruction — The Cognitive Requirements of "Rebuild"

### Johnson-Laird (1983) — Mental Models

**Core claim:** Reasoning does not operate on formal logical rules but on mental models — internal representations that are structurally analogous to the states of affairs they represent. Mental models can be constructed from perception, discourse, or imagination. Reasoning involves: constructing models of premises → drawing conclusions → searching for counterexamples.

Key cognitive requirement: Rebuilding a mental model requires constructing new structural representations, not just updating propositional beliefs. This is more cognitively expensive and more fragile than belief revision.

- Johnson-Laird, P. N. (1983). *Mental Models: Towards a Cognitive Science of Language, Inference, and Consciousness.* Cambridge University Press.

**Status: SUPPORTED** — Johnson-Laird provides the cognitive architecture for what "Rebuild" actually means. Building a new mental model is structurally different from revising a belief — it requires constructing new relational configurations. This validates DCR's use of "Rebuild" as a distinct move, not just "update."

---

### Chi (2008) — Three Types of Conceptual Change

**Core claim:** Conceptual change has three distinct types with different difficulty levels:
1. **Belief revision** — replacing a wrong fact with a right one. Easiest. Occurs when a learner holds a false belief in an otherwise correct framework.
2. **Mental model transformation** — accumulation of multiple belief revisions that collectively shift from a flawed mental model to a correct one. Harder. Requires sustained refutation across multiple related beliefs.
3. **Categorical shift** — reassigning a concept to a different ontological category. Hardest. Required for robust misconceptions where the learner has the right facts in the wrong framework (e.g., treating electric current as a substance rather than a process).

- Chi, M. T. H. (2008). Three types of conceptual change: Belief revision, mental model transformation, and categorical shift. In S. Vosniadou (Ed.), *International Handbook of Research on Conceptual Change* (pp. 61–82). Routledge.

**Status: CRITICAL FINDING FOR DCR** — This taxonomy reveals that DCR's "Rebuild" move is not monolithic. It may require different cognitive operations depending on how deeply wrong or how differently structured the learner's existing model is. For learners with a surface mismatch, belief revision suffices. For learners with a fundamentally different framework, categorical shift is required — and this is resistant to simple comparison instruction. DCR's Rebuild step is most powerful when the prior model is structurally close; it is most limited when categorical revision is needed.

---

### Vosniadou (1992, 2002) — Framework Theory and Constraints on Revision

**Core claim:** Children (and adults) develop framework theories — relatively coherent systems of presuppositions that constrain how new information is interpreted. Misconceptions arise when learners assimilate new information into existing frameworks rather than restructuring the framework. Conceptual change is especially resistant when it requires revision of *framework-level* presuppositions.

Vosniadou & Brewer's research on children's models of the earth showed that apparent inconsistencies were actually the result of *synthetic models* — learners combining scientific information with prior framework constraints (e.g., a "flattened sphere" that satisfies both "the earth is round" and "you can stand on it").

- Vosniadou, S., & Brewer, W. F. (1992). Mental models of the earth: A study of conceptual change in childhood. *Cognitive Psychology, 24*(4), 535–585.

**Status: COMPLICATED** — Vosniadou's framework theory complicates DCR's Rebuild step. Learners don't approach new domains as blank slates — they approach them with pre-existing frameworks that actively constrain what comparisons they can make and what reconstructions are available to them. The "Draw Comparisons" step may be filtered through these frameworks, leading to distorted analogies rather than clean structural mappings. DCR needs a mechanism for surfacing and challenging framework-level assumptions, not just swapping out beliefs.

## Summary Assessment

### What the Evidence Strongly Supports

| Claim | Evidence | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| Draw Comparisons produces transfer | Alfieri meta-analysis (d=0.50), Gentner et al. 2003 | STRONGLY SUPPORTED |
| Experts deconstruct to deep structure | Chi, Feltovich & Glaser 1981 | STRONGLY SUPPORTED |
| 10k-hour rule fails in wicked domains | Macnamara et al. 2014 (<1% variance in professions), Hogarth et al. 2015 | STRONGLY SUPPORTED |
| Wicked environments require different learning strategies | Hogarth 2015, Kahneman & Klein 2009 | STRONGLY SUPPORTED |
| Rebuild requires multiple perspectives and criss-crossing | Spiro et al. CFT | STRONGLY SUPPORTED |
| Analogical reasoning is teachable | Gentner & Loewenstein 2002 | SUPPORTED |
| Metacognitive orientation can be taught | Schraw & Dennison 1994, review literature | SUPPORTED |

### What the Evidence Complicates

| Claim | Complication | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| Experts "draw comparisons" instinctively | Klein RPD shows experts *recognize*, not compare; comparison is a *learning* mechanism, not a real-time performance mechanism | COMPLICATED |
| DCR is equally useful for beginners | CFT: criss-crossing works best at advanced knowledge acquisition stage; novices can be overwhelmed | COMPLICATED |
| Spontaneous cross-domain transfer is learnable | Far transfer is notoriously hard to produce (g=0.11 in meta-analysis); requires extensive scaffolded practice | COMPLICATED |
| Learners can freely "rebuild" models | Vosniadou: framework-level constraints resist revision; existing frameworks filter and distort comparisons | COMPLICATED |
| Breadth = better transfer | Epstein's Range evidence is largely observational; causal mechanism not fully established | COMPLICATED |

### Gaps in the Evidence Base

| Gap | Why It Matters | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| No direct study of DCR as an integrated sequence | Research validates each step separately; no studies test Deconstruct → Compare → Rebuild as a *sequence* | GAP |
| "Wicked domain" boundary conditions are under-specified | Hogarth's framework describes poles; most real domains are mixtures — how mixed is too wicked for RPD to work? | GAP |
| Transfer of DCR orientation across life domains | The claim that DCR is a "practiced orientation" that generalizes reflexively is empirically unvalidated at this specificity | GAP |
| Developmental trajectory of the orientation | At what stage does explicit DCR practice internalize as automatic orientation? No timeline evidence | GAP |
| Individual differences in analogical capacity | Some research (e.g., Goldwater 2021) suggests experts generate richer analogies — but the role of aptitude vs. training in this is unclear | GAP |

## Theoretical Model Summary

The DCR model has a credible and well-supported cognitive science foundation. The three steps map to established constructs:

- **Deconstruct** → Expert problem representation (Chi et al.), schema recognition, deep structure extraction
- **Draw Comparisons** → Analogical encoding (Gentner et al.), structure-mapping, relational abstraction
- **Rebuild** → Mental model construction (Johnson-Laird), cognitive flexibility (Spiro et al.), conceptual change (Chi 2008)

The wicked environment framing is well-grounded in Hogarth and validated by Macnamara's meta-analysis. The teachability claim is credible but requires more scaffolding infrastructure than the doctrine currently specifies.

**The single largest risk in the doctrine's framing** is the assumption that "Draw Comparisons" happens naturally or can be unlocked by prompting. The evidence shows it is the step most likely to fail without deliberate structural support — because retrieval is surface-driven, far transfer is hard, and existing frameworks constrain what comparisons learners can perceive. The comparison step needs to be the *most designed* step in the curriculum, not a bridge between Deconstruct and Rebuild.
