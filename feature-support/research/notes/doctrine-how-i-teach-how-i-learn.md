## Summary

This document is a personal pedagogical philosophy organized into four parts. At its core, the doctrine argues that durable learning in complex, rapidly-shifting environments depends on three interlocking components: a foundation of six transferable "metaskills," a teaching model that scaffolds instruction from theory to application, and a personal learning model designed for rapid domain entry and knowledge transfer. The document presents these not as independent ideas but as a unified architecture, where each layer reinforces the others.

The central argument is that traditional skill-building frameworks — particularly repetition-based mastery models like the 10,000-hour rule — are insufficient in "wicked environments" where context shifts faster than expertise can accumulate. In those environments, what matters is not the depth of a single domain but the underlying cognitive and emotional capacities (metaskills) that allow a person to learn quickly, pattern-match across domains, and rebuild mental models when conditions change. The doctrine claims these capacities can be identified, taught, and developed.

The delivery philosophy rests on the flipped classroom model, which the doctrine positions not merely as a scheduling convenience but as a principled design decision: foundational content (theory and method) is consumed independently before a session, preserving live expert time exclusively for application, adaptive dialogue, and real-world problem-solving. The document asserts this structure is particularly well-suited to adult learners because it accommodates self-direction, leverages existing experience, and respects time constraints. Across all three parts, the stated goal is not producing knowledgeable people but capable people — those who can apply, adapt, and continue learning after formal teaching ends.

## Key Claims (to be validated by research)

1. **The wicked environments claim:** Most skills that matter in work and life exist in "wicked environments" — domains where repetition alone does not produce mastery and where context shifts faster than expertise accumulates. (Implies: the 10,000-hour deliberate practice model has significant domain limits.)

2. **The metaskills claim:** Six specific capacities — Exploring, Creating, Feeling, Imagining, Innovating, Adapting — represent the full range of human cognitive and emotional ability that determines learning effectiveness across contexts. These capacities compound, do not become obsolete, and are teachable.

3. **The pattern-matching claim:** Experts in wicked domains do not start from scratch on novel problems; they deconstruct problems into familiar components, draw cross-domain analogies, and rebuild working models rapidly. This process can be made explicit and taught.

4. **The transfer claim:** A scaffolded instructional sequence (Theory → Method → Application) produces not just knowledge retention but skill transfer — learners who can apply principles to situations they were not trained on.

5. **The flipped classroom claim:** For adult learners, the flipped classroom model produces better outcomes than traditional direct instruction because it redirects scarce expert attention from content delivery (which passive media can handle) to application and adaptive mentoring (which requires live interaction).

6. **The adult learner claim:** Adults are self-directed, experience-driven, and time-constrained, and these characteristics make the flipped model particularly effective for this population. (This aligns with andragogy but the doctrine does not cite Malcolm Knowles or the andragogy literature.)

7. **The integration claim:** The three-layer architecture (metaskills → teaching/learning models → flipped classroom delivery) is more effective as an integrated system than any single component would be in isolation.

8. **The Bloom's adjacency claim:** The Theory → Method → Application teaching sequence "maps cleanly onto Bloom's Taxonomy." This is asserted without elaboration and deserves scrutiny — Bloom's has six levels; the mapping is implied rather than specified.

## Structural Components

**1. The Six Metaskills (Foundation Layer)**
A taxonomy of six transferable capacities claimed to underlie effective learning and performance in complex environments:
- *Exploring* — questioning assumptions, finding root causes, depth-oriented curiosity
- *Creating* — reframing, breaking existing models to make something new
- *Feeling* — empathy, emotional intelligence, reading unspoken signals
- *Imagining* — visualizing futures, possibilities, and scenarios not yet real
- *Innovating* — generating novel solutions under real-world constraints
- *Adapting* — rebuilding mental models in response to changing context and complexity

**2. The Teaching Model: Theory → Method → Application**
A three-stage instructional scaffolding sequence:
- *Theory (The Why)* — foundational concepts, principles, the logic beneath the skill
- *Method (The How)* — a systematic, repeatable process for applying the theory
- *Application (The Now What)* — using the method to solve a real problem in a real context

The sequence is explicitly sequential and directional: understanding before process, process before open-ended practice. The doctrine claims this mirrors how people actually learn, not just how curriculum is often structured.

**3. The Learning Model: Deconstruct → Draw Comparisons → Rebuild**
A three-stage rapid-entry framework for engaging unfamiliar domains or novel problems:
- *Deconstruct (Break It Down)* — identify root structure beneath surface complexity
- *Draw Comparisons (Find the Pattern)* — cross-domain analogical reasoning; what does this resemble?
- *Rebuild (Make It Yours)* — reconstruct a working mental model integrating existing knowledge and new understanding

Explicitly designed for wicked environments where deep repetition-based mastery is not feasible. Described as a "practiced orientation" rather than a procedure — intended to become reflexive.

**4. The Flipped Classroom (Delivery Architecture Layer)**
The structural implementation model:
- Learners engage with foundational content (theory, method) independently before live sessions
- Live session time is reserved exclusively for application, problem-solving, adaptive mentoring, and Socratic dialogue
- The instructor role shifts from content source to facilitator and coach

The doctrine connects this explicitly to both models: independent pre-session work maps to the Theory/Method phases and activates the Deconstruct phase of the learning model; the live session is where Application, Draw Comparisons, and Rebuild occur.

## Internal Logic Assessment

**Where the logic is tight:**

- The relationship between the three layers is coherent and mutually reinforcing. The doctrine does not merely list components — it explains how each layer enables the next.
- The distinction between teaching (delivery) and learning (absorption) is genuinely useful and avoids conflating instructor behavior with learner behavior.
- The argument for protecting expert time in the flipped model is practically grounded and follows logically from the premise that content is abundant but expert attention is scarce.
- The mapping of Deconstruct to the Exploring metaskill and Rebuild to the Adapting metaskill is internally consistent.

**Where the logic has gaps or unstated assumptions:**

- The six metaskills are presented as comprehensive ("the full range of human cognitive and emotional capacity") but the basis for this claim is not established. Why these six? What framework generated them? The doctrine asserts completeness without demonstrating it.
- The claim that metaskills "compound" is suggestive but unspecified — compound at what rate, under what conditions, with what interventions? This is the kind of claim that needs empirical grounding.
- The Bloom's Taxonomy reference is invoked but not developed. The doctrine says Theory → Method → Application "maps cleanly" onto Bloom's without specifying which levels correspond to which stages or acknowledging that Bloom's is a taxonomy of cognitive objectives, not a teaching sequence.
- "Wicked environments" is used as a central premise without definition or citation. The concept originates with Rittel and Webber (1973) and was extended by Robin Hogarth for learning environments — but neither is cited. The doctrine borrows the framing without acknowledging its source.
- The doctrine claims flipped classroom research is "solid, particularly for adult learners" without citing any of it. This is the most testable empirical claim in the document and the one most in need of validation.
- The Learning Model (Deconstruct → Compare → Rebuild) is described as what "fast learners in complex environments tend to do reflexively" — but no evidence base is offered for this behavioral description. It reads as observed-but-unverified.
- The doctrine asserts that the Teaching Model "implicitly builds the Metaskills" during instruction, but the mechanism is not explained. How does moving a learner through Theory → Application develop, say, Imagining or Feeling?
