# Transfer of Training: Baldwin & Ford Model, Blume et al. Meta-Analysis, and Transfer Rates

**Research question:** What does the training transfer literature say about whether compressed/intensive training formats actually produce durable workplace behavior change? What are the documented transfer rates, and what factors predict successful transfer?

**Date:** 2026-03-15
**Researcher:** Claude (claude-opus-4-6)
**Method:** Tavily advanced search across transfer of training research, Baldwin & Ford model, Blume et al. meta-analysis, with source extraction

---

## Summary Assessment

The transfer of training literature presents a sobering picture for any compressed learning format. The foundational estimate from Baldwin & Ford (1988) — that less than 10% of training produces behavioral change on the job — has been widely cited for decades. More recent research (Saks, 2013; Blume et al., 2010) adds nuance but does not dramatically improve the picture. The key finding for this project: training design alone is insufficient for transfer; work environment and individual characteristics are equally or more important. A curriculum builder that generates content without addressing transfer conditions is likely producing content that will follow the typical pattern — temporary engagement without durable change.

## 1. Baldwin & Ford (1988): The Foundational Transfer Model

**Tag: SUPPORTED — foundational framework**

**Source:** Baldwin, T.T. & Ford, J.K. (1988). Transfer of training: A review and directions for future research. *Personnel Psychology*, 41(1), 63-105.
**URLs:**
- https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/1988-baldwin.pdf
- https://flip.tools/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/01.-Transfer-of-training-1988_Baldwin-Ford.pdf

Baldwin & Ford analyzed 63 empirical studies (1907-1987) and organized transfer around three categories of inputs:

1. **Trainee characteristics** — ability, skill, motivation, personality (need for achievement, locus of control, general intelligence)
2. **Training design** — objectives, methods, incorporation of learning principles (identical elements, stimulus variability, general principles, overlearning)
3. **Work environment** — supervisory support, opportunity to perform, transfer climate

These inputs produce **training outputs** (knowledge/skill acquisition during training), which then feed into **conditions of transfer** (generalization to job + maintenance over time).

### Key Claims with Data

- **The "less than 10%" estimate:** Baldwin & Ford (1988) estimated that less than 10% of training expenditure resulted in behavioral change that transferred to the job. This became the most-cited statistic in the field. *However* — Holton & Baldwin (2000) later noted this figure "has not been empirically documented" as a precise number; it was an estimate based on the pattern of evidence, not a measured rate. [Source: files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED504338.pdf]

- **Supervisory support is critical:** Employees who perceived training as important to their supervisor were more motivated to attend, learn, and transfer. Huczynski & Lewis (1980) and Fleishman (1953) studies showed that trainees returning to supervisors high in consideration exhibited more of the trained behaviors; those returning to low-consideration supervisors did not.

- **Identical elements principle:** Transfer maximized when stimulus and response elements in training match the actual work environment. This has implications for compressed curricula — if the training context is a Zoom room and the work context is a factory floor, transfer suffers regardless of content quality.

- **General principles teaching:** Transfer is facilitated when trainees are taught not just skills but the underlying rules and theoretical principles. This directly supports the doctrine's Theory → Method → Application sequence as a transfer-enhancing design.

### Implications for Curriculum Builder

A curriculum builder tool that generates only content (the "training design" input) is addressing only one of three necessary conditions for transfer. The other two — trainee characteristics and work environment — are outside the tool's direct control. However, the tool *can* design transfer-supporting structures into the curriculum itself: pre-work that assesses motivation, post-session application tasks that bridge to the work environment, and supervisor involvement prompts.

## 2. Blume, Ford, Baldwin & Huang (2010): Meta-Analytic Review

**Tag: COMPLICATED — evidence is weaker than expected**

**Source:** Blume, B.D., Ford, J.K., Baldwin, T.T., & Huang, J.L. (2010). Transfer of training: A meta-analytic review. *Journal of Management*, 36(4), 1065-1105.
**URLs:**
- http://jasonhuangatwork.com/papers/Blume_Ford_Baldwin_Huang_2010_JOM-Training_Transfer_Meta.pdf
- https://moodle.unige.ch/pluginfile.php/2325603/mod_folder/content/0/4-%20Transfert-Impact/

This meta-analysis reviewed 89 empirical studies (1988-2008) to quantify what the Baldwin & Ford model predicted qualitatively.

### Key Claims with Data

- **Transfer interventions are less effective than assumed:** "Put simply, the evidence in support of transfer interventions was not as compelling as either our intuition or prior transfer commentaries would suggest." This is a direct quote from the meta-analysis — significant because it means even deliberate attempts to enhance transfer often fall short.

- **Open vs. closed skills distinction matters enormously:** Transfer correlations are substantially higher for "closed" skills (specific, procedural tasks with clear right/wrong answers) than for "open" skills (interpersonal, leadership, adaptive behaviors). Most compressed executive/professional programs teach open skills — which are precisely the hardest to transfer.

- **Self-report bias inflates transfer estimates:** When learners rate both their own learning and their own transfer, correlations appear much larger than when objective measures or third-party assessments are used. This means the completion rates and satisfaction scores that cohort-based programs advertise are almost certainly overestimating actual transfer.

- **Cognitive ability and self-efficacy** showed moderate relationships with transfer. Motivation had a weaker-than-expected relationship once other variables were controlled.

- **Work environment factors** (supervisor support, transfer climate, opportunity to perform) showed consistent positive relationships with transfer, but the meta-analysis could not differentiate between different types of supervisor support behaviors because studies operationalized them differently.

### Key Methodological Finding

The researchers noted massive variation in how "transfer" was measured across studies — some measured knowledge retention, some measured behavior change, some measured performance outcomes. This measurement inconsistency means there is no reliable single "transfer rate" number; the oft-cited "10%" is a rough heuristic, not a measured quantity.

## 3. Saks (2013) and Post-Training Transfer Decay

**Tag: SUPPORTED — decay pattern is well-documented**

**Source:** Cited in Fagan (Walden University dissertation, 2020), referencing Saks, A.M. (2013).
**URL:** https://scholarworks.waldenu.edu/context/dissertations/article/5901/viewcontent/Fagan_waldenu_0543D_20009.pdf

### Key Data Point

- **Saks (2013) found:** Immediately after training, 40% of employees failed to transfer skills taught. Within 1 year, this number jumped to 70%.
- Multiple other scholars corroborated low transfer rates: Ford et al. (2011), Ng (2013), Prieto & Phipps (2011).
- Swanson & Holton (2001) estimated that a medical technician completing training would retain only about 50% of relevant knowledge after 4 years — and suggested business/other professions are not immune to this erosion.

### Transfer Decay Pattern

The evidence suggests a consistent pattern: even when initial transfer occurs, it decays over time unless actively maintained. This has critical implications for compressed formats — a 4-week cohort course may produce strong immediate results that appear to validate the format, but without follow-up support structures, the transfer will erode.

## 4. Grossman & Salas (2011): What Really Matters for Transfer

**Tag: SUPPORTED — strongest predictors identified**

**Source:** Grossman, R. & Salas, E. (2011). The transfer of training: What really matters. *International Journal of Training and Development*, 15(2), 103-120.
**Cited in:** https://www.scirp.org/pdf/ojbm_2022111715381601.pdf

Using Baldwin & Ford's model as framework, Grossman & Salas identified the factors with the **strongest, most consistent** relationships with transfer:

### Trainee Characteristics (strongest predictors)
- Cognitive ability
- Self-efficacy
- Motivation to transfer
- Perceived utility of training

### Training Design (strongest predictors)
- Behavioral modeling (showing the behavior, not just describing it)
- Error management training (practicing with mistakes built in)
- Realistic training environments (fidelity to the actual work context)

### Work Environment (strongest predictors)
- Transfer climate (organizational culture supports application)
- Supervisor and peer support
- Opportunity to perform (chances to use the skills on the job)
- Follow-up activities

### Implications for Curriculum Builder

The Grossman & Salas findings create a design checklist that a curriculum builder should encode:
1. Does the curriculum include behavioral modeling (demonstrations, not just explanations)?
2. Does it include error management (practice scenarios where things go wrong)?
3. Is the training context realistic to the work context?
4. Does it include post-program follow-up structures?
5. Does it address supervisor/manager involvement?

A tool that generates curricula without items 4 and 5 is designing for temporary engagement, not transfer.

## 5. Work-Learning Research (Thalheimer, 2020): Practitioner Review

**Tag: SUPPORTED — practical synthesis of research**

**Source:** Thalheimer, W. (2020). Transfer of training: Quick research review.
**URL:** https://www.worklearning.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/Transfer-of-Training-Quick-Research-Review-2020-v1a.pdf

### Key Findings (lettered for reference)

- **A.** The transfer problem remains acute despite decades of research.
- **B.** Ford, Baldwin & Prasad (2018) noted the number of transfer studies has "exploded" but acknowledged "legitimate concern that the transfer problem remains acute and there is so much more of value that remains unknown."
- **C.** Learners with supervisors who encourage, support, and monitor transfer are more likely to succeed (Blume et al., 2010).
- **D.** Govaerts & Dochy (2014) identified 24 different means supervisors could use to support transfer — showing the complexity of what "supervisor support" actually means.
- **E.** Self-efficacy can decay over time (Blume et al., 2010), meaning early training gains don't automatically persist.
- **F.** Early transfer success breeds more transfer — learners who succeed in first application attempts persevere more (Blume, Ford, Surface & Olenick, 2019; Huang, Ford & Ryan, 2017).
- **G.** Same-source bias significantly inflates transfer estimates — when learners rate their own transfer, numbers look better than reality.

### Practical Translation

Thalheimer noted a gap between research and practice — transfer researchers generate findings, but practitioners rarely receive actionable guidance. This is precisely the opportunity a curriculum builder tool addresses: encoding research-validated transfer principles into the generation process so practitioners don't need to know the literature to benefit from it.

## 6. Accelerated vs. Compressed Learning Distinction

**Tag: COMPLICATED — terminology matters**

**Source:** Andrews & Fitzgerald (2010), cited in DTIC accelerated learning review.
**URL:** https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/tr/pdf/ADA574124.pdf

### Critical Distinction

- **Accelerated learning:** The typical timeframe is shortened (e.g., 4-10 weeks vs. 16-20 weeks) AND the total learning hours are reduced (e.g., 20-32 hours vs. 48-60 hours). Same material learned in less time with fewer hours.
- **Compressed courses:** Learning is intensified. Students spend the same total time (40-45 hours) in a shorter calendar window (6-8 weeks vs. 16 weeks).

Most "cohort-based courses" and bootcamps are *compressed*, not truly accelerated. They pack the same or more hours into a shorter calendar period. The distinction matters because the research on each format has different findings — compressed courses can maintain learning quality if designed well, while truly accelerated courses face steeper trade-offs.

### Additional Finding from DTIC Review

Individual differences explain substantial variance in transfer:
- Motivation, personality, age, and situational variables together explained 81% of variance in transfer outcomes.
- When only proximal variables (learning outcomes) were considered, only 50% of variance was explained.
- This means individual attributes have a direct impact on transfer *independent of training quality*.

## Cross-Cutting Findings

### What Predicts Durable Outcomes (across all sources)

1. **Realistic practice conditions** that match the work environment
2. **Supervisor support** before, during, and after training
3. **Opportunity to perform** — chances to use new skills shortly after training
4. **Transfer climate** — organizational culture that values application
5. **Follow-up structures** — post-training reinforcement activities
6. **Early success experiences** — initial application attempts that succeed breed persistence
7. **Teaching underlying principles** — not just skills, but *why* the skills work (supports the TMA sequence)

### What Predicts Temporary Engagement Only

1. High satisfaction scores without behavioral measurement (smile sheets)
2. Self-reported transfer without third-party validation
3. No post-training support structures
4. Return to unsupportive work environment
5. Training context disconnected from work context
6. Skills taught as closed procedures when actual work requires open/adaptive application

### Transfer Rate Summary

| Claim | Source | Confidence |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Less than 10% of training produces behavioral change | Baldwin & Ford (1988) estimate | Moderate — widely cited but not empirically measured as a precise number |
| 40% fail to transfer immediately; 70% within 1 year | Saks (2013) | Moderate — single study but consistent with pattern |
| 50% knowledge retention after 4 years | Swanson & Holton (2001) | Moderate — domain-specific (medical) |
| Transfer interventions less effective than assumed | Blume et al. (2010) meta-analysis | High — meta-analytic finding |
| Open skills transfer far less than closed skills | Blume et al. (2010) | High — meta-analytic finding |

## Implications for Curriculum Builder Tool

1. **A curriculum builder that generates only content is insufficient.** Transfer requires design for the work environment, not just the classroom. The tool must generate transfer support structures (pre-work, post-session application tasks, manager briefings, follow-up check-ins).

2. **The tool should distinguish open vs. closed skill training.** Closed-skill curricula (procedures, technical operations) can rely more on practice and repetition. Open-skill curricula (leadership, communication, strategy) need realistic practice contexts, error management, and ongoing coaching support.

3. **Self-reported outcomes are unreliable proxies for transfer.** The tool should generate evaluation structures that go beyond satisfaction surveys — behavioral observation checklists, 360 feedback prompts, on-the-job application logs.

4. **Compressed format is not inherently inferior** but requires explicit transfer design to overcome the decay pattern. Without it, compressed programs produce the exact pattern the doctrine is trying to avoid: temporary motivation without durable skill change.

5. **The "10% transfer rate" is a useful provocation, not a precise measurement.** The tool's value proposition should be framed around *improving transfer by design*, not citing a specific failure rate.
