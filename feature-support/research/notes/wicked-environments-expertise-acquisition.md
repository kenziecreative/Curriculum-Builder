# Wicked Learning Environments & Expertise Acquisition
## Expertise Beyond Kind Domains — Implications for DCR and Curriculum Design

**Research Note | March 2026**
**Purpose:** Assess how expertise develops in wicked learning environments (management, consulting, creative work, medicine, technology), test the limits of deliberate practice in such domains, evaluate alternative models (naturalistic decision-making, adaptive expertise), and determine whether Deconstruct→Compare→Rebuild maps to how experts actually develop in wicked environments.

## 1. The Kind/Wicked Framework — What It Actually Says

### Hogarth's Original Framework (2001, formalized 2015)

Robin Hogarth introduced the kind/wicked distinction in *Educating Intuition* (2001), later formalized with Lejarraga and Soyer (2015). The core claim is about the conditions under which learning from experience leads to accurate intuitions.

**Kind learning environments** provide feedback that is (a) accurate, (b) complete, (c) timely, and (d) directly linked to the action or judgment that produced it. Chess, golf, and music are canonical examples. In kind environments, repetition builds valid expertise — the 10,000-hour paradigm works.

**Wicked learning environments** provide feedback that is (a) delayed, (b) ambiguous, (c) incomplete, or (d) systematically misleading. Rules may shift. Patterns don't repeat reliably. The same action can produce different outcomes depending on context. Business, policy, investing, leadership, and complex medical diagnosis are wicked domains.

**Critical distinction Hogarth never made, but the Commoncog analysis clarifies:** Kind and wicked describe *learning structures*, not immutable properties of domains. A domain can be wicked (investing) but a practitioner can construct kinder learning structures within it. Conversely, a kind domain (medicine, in that diseases have causal mechanisms) can produce wicked learning structures if feedback is unavailable or delayed. This distinction — structures vs. domains — is essential for curriculum design.

**Source: [Hogarth et al. 2015 PDF](https://preview-prod-production.gurooproducer.com/learntolead2021/hogarth-et-al-2015.pdf); [Commoncog analysis](https://commoncog.com/ill-structured-domains-not-wicked/)**

**Status: SUPPORTED** — The kind/wicked framework is empirically grounded and widely cited (Kahneman & Klein 2009 agreed on this as the key variable). It directly validates the thesis assumption that entrepreneurship, management, and creative work are wicked domains where conventional expertise-building fails.

**Status: COMPLICATED** — The thesis treats wickedness as a fixed property of the operating domain. The evidence shows it's a property of the *learning structure*, which means curriculum design can make wicked domains less wicked for learning purposes. This is actually *more optimistic* for the project than the doctrine assumes, but requires a design reframe.

---

### The Typhoid Mary Illustration

Hogarth highlighted a famous New York City physician renowned for diagnosing typhoid fever by feeling patients' tongues. He repeatedly diagnosed cases before symptoms appeared. Another physician later discovered: "He was a more productive carrier, using only his hands, than Typhoid Mary." Repetitive success in a wicked learning environment taught him the worst possible lesson — his feedback loop was systematically misleading.

**Implication for curriculum design:** Wicked environments can produce confident-but-wrong practitioners. Curriculum for wicked domains must build in mechanisms to check whether feedback loops are valid, not just whether learners are gaining confidence.

## 2. Deliberate Practice Limitations in Wicked Environments

### What Ericsson's Model Requires

Ericsson, Krampe & Tesch-Romer (1993) defined deliberate practice as requiring:
- Well-defined tasks practiced at appropriate difficulty
- Informative, immediate feedback to monitor improvement
- Opportunities for repetition and error correction
- Access to teachers who can design practice activities

The Ericsson model works well in **kind environments** where these conditions are naturally met: music, chess, sports, typing. Performance in these domains correlates strongly with accumulated deliberate practice hours.

**Source: [Ericsson et al. 1993 PDF](https://graphics8.nytimes.com/images/blogs/freakonomics/pdf/DeliberatePractice(PsychologicalReview).pdf); [Royal Society replication review 2019](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsos.190327)**

### Where Deliberate Practice Breaks Down

In wicked environments, Ericsson's prerequisites systematically fail:

1. **Feedback is delayed or misleading.** A venture capital investment takes 5-10 years to produce an outcome. An organizational restructure takes 6-18 months to show effects. A leadership decision's impact is entangled with dozens of confounding variables.

2. **Tasks are not well-defined.** Unlike playing scales at increasing tempo, the "task" in management, consulting, or creative work shifts with every engagement. There is no stable performance target to practice toward.

3. **Repetition is unreliable.** The same negotiation tactic that worked in one context may fail in another. Surface similarity between situations masks structural differences.

4. **Outcomes reflect luck as much as skill.** In domains with high variance, even experts struggle to distinguish signal from noise in their feedback.

As the practitioner synthesis (Hughes-Jones) notes: "Much of business, leadership and life doesn't take place in the types of kind learning environments described above. It takes place in wicked learning environments, that don't lend themselves well to rote teaching and practice."

**Status: SUPPORTED** — The thesis claim that the 10,000-hour model breaks down in entrepreneurship and adjacent domains is well-supported. Deliberate practice remains useful for *foundational skills* (writing, presenting, basic analytics) but fails as a model for developing *domain judgment* in wicked environments.

**Status: COMPLICATED** — Deliberate practice is still valuable *earlier* in a career for building the procedural base on which higher-order judgment rests. The thesis shouldn't dismiss it entirely — it should position it as necessary-but-insufficient for wicked domain expertise.

## 3. Alternative Models for Wicked-Domain Expertise

### Klein's Recognition-Primed Decision Model (RPD)

Gary Klein's naturalistic decision-making (NDM) research studied how firefighters, military officers, ICU nurses, and other practitioners make decisions under time pressure, uncertainty, and incomplete information. The RPD model (Klein 1993, 1998) describes how experienced practitioners:

1. **Recognize the situation** by matching it to patterns from prior experience
2. **Generate a single plausible course of action** (not comparing options)
3. **Mentally simulate** that action to check for problems
4. **Modify or proceed** based on the simulation

RPD differs from rational-choice decision-making (compare all options, pick the best) in that experts rarely compare options. They recognize the type of situation, retrieve a workable response, and adapt it. Klein found that firefighters used RPD in about 80% of decisions.

**Key qualification from Commoncog analysis:** RPD describes how experts use *already-developed* tacit knowledge. It does not describe how that knowledge is acquired. Practitioners who lack pattern recognition fall back to deliberate comparison — which is slow, but is how novices *must* operate until they develop sufficient experience.

Klein notes that practitioners who are never given the opportunity to train their intuitions — who only use deliberate analysis — have their learning rate "forever stunted."

**Source: [Commoncog RPD analysis](https://commoncog.com/putting-mental-models-to-practice/); Klein, G. (1998). *Sources of Power: How People Make Decisions.*; [Intuition as competence paper](https://asianonlinejournals.com/index.php/JEELR/article/download/7883/3368/12331)**

**Status: SUPPORTED** — RPD validates the thesis claim that expert performance in wicked domains relies on pattern recognition and analogical matching, not systematic analysis. This is the cognitive mechanism that DCR's "Deconstruct" and "Draw Comparisons" moves aim to accelerate.

**Status: COMPLICATED** — RPD requires *valid cue environments* — situations where the patterns being recognized correspond to real causal structures. In truly wicked environments (where feedback is systematically misleading), RPD can produce confident-but-wrong experts, just like the typhoid doctor. Curriculum design must address cue validity, not just pattern exposure.

### Hatano & Inagaki's Adaptive vs. Routine Expertise (1986)

Hatano and Inagaki distinguished two developmental paths for expertise:

**Routine expertise:** High efficiency in performing procedural skills. Routine experts are "outstanding in terms of speed, accuracy, and automaticity of performance, but lack flexibility and adaptability to new problems." They can only apply skills in familiar contexts. An abacus master who can calculate rapidly but cannot explain the principles behind the calculations, and who cannot adapt when the problem format changes.

**Adaptive expertise:** Combines procedural efficiency with deep conceptual understanding. Adaptive experts "understand the meaning of the skills" and can "modify or invent skills according to local constraints." They can transfer to novel situations, explain their reasoning, and judge when conventional approaches are inappropriate.

**Three conditions that promote adaptive expertise over routine expertise:**

1. **Variability in the learning environment.** Encountering sufficient variation — not just repetition — forces learners to develop conceptual understanding rather than relying on pattern-matched procedures. Hatano and Inagaki proposed that people need to experience enough variability to support active experimentation.

2. **Understanding-oriented culture vs. promptitude-oriented culture.** If the environment values speed and correct execution (promptitude), learners develop routine expertise. If the environment values understanding *why* (even at the cost of speed), adaptive expertise develops. "Accumulated practice of procedural skill is likely to lead to adaptive expertise under the understanding-oriented culture, while to routine expertise under the promptitude-oriented culture."

3. **Active experimentation.** Learners who actively experiment with their environment — testing boundaries, trying variations, explaining outcomes — develop adaptive expertise. Those who simply repeat prescribed procedures develop routine expertise.

**Medical education application:** Recent work (Mylopoulos et al. 2018; Grierson & Vanstone 2021) has found adaptive expertise "increasingly relevant because seeking out new information to inform an adapted approach is fundamental when clinicians manage patients with complex, undifferentiated, and/or ambiguous health concerns." This maps directly to wicked environments in medicine.

**Source: [Hatano & Inagaki 1986 original](https://eprints.lib.hokudai.ac.jp/repo/huscap/all/25206/6_P27-36.pdf); [Stanford adaptive expertise paper](https://aaalab.stanford.edu/assets/papers/2007/Intercultural_adaptive_expertise.pdf); [Re-examining routine and adaptive expertise](https://centreforfacdev.ca/pdf/TLC%20Resources/Re-examining%20the%20Integration%20of%20Routine%20and%20Adaptive%20Expertise.pdf); [Routine and Adaptive Experts review](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1459947.pdf)**

**Status: STRONGLY SUPPORTED** — The adaptive/routine distinction maps powerfully onto the thesis goals. The curriculum builder should produce curricula that develop *adaptive* expertise, not routine expertise. This means:
- Deliberate variation in case types (not repetition of the same exercise)
- Explicit conceptual understanding alongside procedural skill
- Active experimentation, not just consumption
- Culture of "why" over culture of speed

**Status: COMPLICATED** — Hatano and Inagaki describe adaptive expertise as *domain-specific* because it develops from accumulated domain experience. This complicates DCR's claim of rapid *cross-domain* entry. Adaptive expertise in a new domain still requires substantial experience within that domain — comparison across domains accelerates schema formation but may not shortcut the experience requirement.

## 4. Analogical Reasoning and DCR in Wicked Environments

### Does DCR Map to How Experts Actually Develop?

The DCR model (Deconstruct→Draw Comparisons→Rebuild) claims that expert practitioners in wicked domains:
1. Break down exemplars into structural components (Deconstruct)
2. Compare across domains to find transferable patterns (Draw Comparisons)
3. Rebuild a working approach in their own context (Rebuild)

The cognitive science evidence from analogical reasoning research provides strong but qualified support.

### What the Evidence Supports

**Analogical encoding works.** Gentner, Loewenstein & Thompson (2003) demonstrated across three experiments that comparing two cases simultaneously produces better schema abstraction and transfer than studying cases individually. In negotiation contexts, graduate management students who compared two cases were *nearly three times more likely* to transfer the strategy to live negotiations. This directly supports the "Draw Comparisons" move.

**Schema abstraction is the mechanism.** When learners compare two structurally similar but surface-dissimilar cases, they extract the underlying relational structure — a schema — that can then be applied to new situations. This is what DCR's "Deconstruct" and "Draw Comparisons" aim to produce.

**Cross-domain transfer is possible but conditional.** Gentner and Smith (2012) showed that experts in a domain are more likely than novices to retrieve relationally similar examples. But even experts retrieve some surface-similar examples. Novick (1988) found experts reject misleading surface matches faster. There is "an unfortunate dissociation: while accuracy of transfer depends critically on the degree of relational pattern matching, memory retrieval depends largely on surface similarity between domains."

**Source: [Gentner & Maravilla 2018 handbook chapter](https://groups.psych.northwestern.edu/gentner/papers/GentnerMaravilla_2018-Handbook.pdf); [Gentner & Smith 2012](https://groups.psych.northwestern.edu/gentner/papers/gentnerSmith_2012.pdf); [Gentner, Loewenstein & Thompson 2003](https://groups.psych.northwestern.edu/gentner/papers/GentnerLoewensteinThompson03.pdf); [Loewenstein, Thompson & Gentner 1999](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.3758/BF03212967.pdf)**

### What Complicates DCR as a Rapid Domain-Entry Framework

**1. The retrieval problem.** Spontaneous analogical transfer is rare in laboratory settings (Gick & Holyoak 1980, 1983). People who learn a principle from one case frequently fail to apply it to a structurally similar but surface-different case. The "inert knowledge problem" — knowing something but failing to retrieve it when relevant — is a fundamental challenge. DCR must explicitly scaffold retrieval, not assume it happens naturally.

**2. Novice-expert asymmetry.** Novices retrieve analogs based on surface similarity; experts retrieve based on structural similarity. This means DCR's "Draw Comparisons" move is most effective for people who already have some domain expertise — and least reliable for the true novices who would benefit most from cross-domain transfer. Curriculum design must scaffold comparison for novices through explicit structural cues.

**3. Case selection is critical.** Alfieri, Nokes-Malach & Schunn's (2013) meta-analysis showed comparison-based learning has a medium effect size (d = 0.50), but only when compared cases share underlying structure while differing on surface features. Poorly chosen case pairs — too similar, too different, or structurally unrelated — can fail or backfire. The curriculum builder must encode case-selection criteria.

**4. Within-domain exemplars can constrain creativity.** Ball & Schunn's engineering design research showed that when designers had access to within-domain prototypes, they made far fewer between-domain analogies. Within-domain exemplars provide "paths of least resistance" that constrain creative thinking. This suggests DCR should deliberately include *cross-domain* exemplars, not just within-domain ones, to avoid fixation.

**5. Transfer requires explicit structural alignment.** Vendetti et al. (2015) found that spontaneous cross-domain transfer is rare without instructional support. Learners need to be explicitly prompted to look past surface differences and identify shared relational structure. Mere exposure to multiple examples is insufficient — the comparison must be active and guided.

**Status: SUPPORTED** — DCR's cognitive mechanism (structural comparison → schema abstraction → transfer) is well-validated by Gentner's research program. The "Draw Comparisons" move has strong empirical support.

**Status: COMPLICATED** — DCR as a *rapid domain-entry framework for novices in wicked environments* faces three specific challenges: (a) novices are worst at the comparison move the model relies on, (b) spontaneous transfer is unreliable without scaffolding, and (c) wicked environments add noise to case interpretation even after transfer occurs. DCR works, but requires heavy instructional scaffolding that the doctrine doesn't currently specify.

## 5. Synthesis: Implications for Curriculum Design in Wicked Domains

### What Must Be True for DCR-Based Curricula in Wicked Domains

Based on the evidence reviewed, a curriculum builder targeting wicked domains must encode the following principles:

**1. Make the wicked kind (where possible).** Following Hogarth's insight that wickedness is a property of learning structures, not just domains, curricula should construct kinder learning structures within wicked domains. This means:
- Compress feedback loops (simulations, rapid case analysis, structured peer review)
- Make causal mechanisms explicit rather than leaving them to be inferred from noisy experience
- Flag when feedback is likely misleading vs. valid

**2. Design for adaptive, not routine expertise.** Following Hatano and Inagaki:
- Build in deliberate variation across cases and exercises
- Require conceptual explanation alongside procedural execution
- Create an understanding-oriented culture (ask "why does this work?" not just "does this work?")
- Include active experimentation, not just consumption

**3. Scaffold the comparison move for novices.** Following Gentner's analogical encoding research:
- Provide explicitly paired cases for comparison (not single cases studied in isolation)
- Include cross-domain exemplars to prevent fixation on within-domain patterns
- Use structural cues to guide novices toward relational rather than surface matching
- Progressive alignment: start with close comparisons, move to distant ones as schemas develop

**4. Build cue-validity checks into the curriculum.** Following Klein's RPD and Hogarth's typhoid doctor warning:
- Teach learners to assess whether their feedback loops are valid
- Include deliberate exposure to cases where intuition fails
- Build metacognitive checks: "Am I learning the right lesson from this experience?"

**5. DCR needs a "Validate" step.** The current Deconstruct→Compare→Rebuild model lacks an explicit mechanism for validating whether the rebuilt approach works in the target context. In wicked environments, this is not optional — it's where the model is most vulnerable. A fourth step (Validate/Test) should be encoded.

**6. Position deliberate practice appropriately.** Deliberate practice builds the procedural foundation (early career, foundational skills). DCR and analogical reasoning build the adaptive expertise layer on top. Curriculum should sequence accordingly: procedural foundations first, then cross-domain comparison and adaptive problem-solving.

## Gaps Identified

- **GAP:** No direct empirical study testing DCR as a named framework in wicked professional domains. The evidence supports the components (analogical encoding, structural comparison, adaptive expertise) but not the specific composite model.
- **GAP:** Limited evidence on how quickly analogical encoding can accelerate domain entry for true novices in wicked environments. Most studies use participants with some prior knowledge.
- **GAP:** Hatano and Inagaki's "Imagining" metaskill equivalent — the capacity for generative hypothesizing in novel situations — has limited empirical treatment in the expertise literature. How this maps to wicked-domain expertise development is underspecified.
- **GAP:** No curriculum design framework specifically synthesizes Hogarth + Hatano + Gentner + Klein into a coherent design methodology. This may be the white space the project occupies.
