# Experiential Learning Cycles and Transformative Learning

**Research Questions**: (1) What does research on experiential learning cycles (Kolb, Honey & Mumford, 5E/BSCS) say about evidence-based session design — which phases are most commonly skipped? (2) What does research on transformative learning (Mezirow) say about perspective transformation in adult education?

**Date**: 2026-03-15
**Finding Tags**: SUPPORTED (learning cycles), COMPLICATED (implementation fidelity), SUPPORTED (transformative learning theory)

---

## 1. Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle

**Source**: David Kolb, *Experiential Learning: Experience as the Source of Learning and Development* (1984)
**Systematic review**: Morris, T.H. (2020), "Experiential learning – a systematic review and revision of Kolb's model," *Interactive Learning Environments*, 28(8)

### The Four Phases

1. **Concrete Experience** — Direct engagement with a task or phenomenon
2. **Reflective Observation** — Stepping back to observe and reflect on the experience
3. **Abstract Conceptualization** — Drawing general principles, theories, or models from reflection
4. **Active Experimentation** — Testing those principles in new situations

### Key Findings

- **Implementation fidelity is poor**: A 2025 systematic review (ScienceDirect) of 30 studies found that only 2 (6.7%) fully adhered to all guidelines of Kolb's cycle. Only 30% (n=9) fully implemented the model. Most studies skipped or underweighted at least one phase.
- **Morris (2020)** systematic review: Kolb's model is widely used but rarely implemented completely; the most commonly truncated phases are Reflective Observation and Abstract Conceptualization — practitioners tend to jump from experience directly to experimentation.
- **Honey & Mumford learning styles** (built on Kolb): The learning styles component has been widely criticized — Coffield et al. (2004) review found insufficient empirical evidence for learning style matching improving outcomes. The cycle structure is more defensible than the styles typology.

### Which Phases Get Skipped

The evidence consistently shows:
- **Reflective Observation** is the most commonly underweighted phase — practitioners rush from doing to doing more, without structured reflection
- **Abstract Conceptualization** is the second most commonly truncated — practitioners skip the "what's the general principle here?" step
- This maps to a predict: practitioners favor action phases (Concrete Experience, Active Experimentation) over thinking phases (Reflection, Conceptualization)

### Implications for Curriculum Builder

- Any session design template should make reflection and conceptualization REQUIRED, not optional — because they are the phases that naturally get dropped
- Schema enforcement (from Phase 5 findings) is relevant here: if reflection is merely suggested, it will be omitted; if it's a required field, it will be generated

## 2. The 5E Model — Meta-Analytic Evidence

**Source**: Bybee et al. (1987), BSCS; Polanin, Austin, Taylor, Steingut, Rodgers, & Williams (2024), "Effects of the 5E Instructional Model: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis," *AERA Open*

### Effect Size Data

- **Science outcomes**: g = 0.82 (95% CI [0.67, 0.97]) — large effect, but high heterogeneity
- **Math**: Only 6 eligible studies — insufficient for confirmatory moderator analysis
- **Motivation**: Supplementary outcome; exploratory analyses only
- **5E vs. 7E**: 7E model (g = 1.23) significantly larger effects than 3E model (g = 0.42)
- **Sample**: 61 RCTs, 156 effect sizes; 70% met WWC standards

### The 7E Extension (Eisenkraft, 2003)

Added "Elicit" (before Engage — explicitly surface prior conceptions) and "Extend" (after Elaborate — far transfer). The larger effect size for 7E suggests that making prior-knowledge elicitation and transfer explicit matters.

### Implementation Fidelity Gaps

- The Elaborate and Evaluate phases are most commonly underimplemented
- Teachers tend to emphasize Engage and Explain, truncating the phases that require student-driven application and assessment
- This mirrors Kolb: action-oriented and presentation phases get done; reflection, application, and evaluation phases get cut

## 3. Mezirow's Transformative Learning Theory

**Source**: Jack Mezirow, "Transformative Dimensions of Adult Learning" (1991); "Learning as Transformation" (2000)

### The Core Mechanism

Transformative learning occurs through **perspective transformation** — a fundamental shift in the learner's meaning structures (frames of reference, mental models, assumptions). The trigger is a **disorienting dilemma** — an experience that cannot be assimilated into existing meaning structures and forces reconstruction.

### Mezirow's 10 Phases of Perspective Transformation

1. A disorienting dilemma
2. Self-examination with feelings of guilt or shame
3. A critical assessment of assumptions
4. Recognition that one's discontent and the process of transformation are shared
5. Exploration of options for new roles, relationships, and actions
6. Planning a course of action
7. Acquiring knowledge and skills for implementing plans
8. Provisional trying of new roles
9. Building competence and self-confidence in new roles and relationships
10. A reintegration into one's life on the basis of conditions dictated by one's new perspective

### Encoding "Aha Moments" More Rigorously

The old template's Step 7 ("Generate 'Aha' Ideas") treated belief shifts as marketing content to brainstorm. Mezirow's framework provides a more rigorous structure:

- **Aha moments are not content — they are cognitive events** triggered by specific pedagogical conditions
- **The trigger must be a genuine disorienting dilemma**, not a clever reframe — the learner must encounter something that their existing mental model cannot explain
- **Belief shifts require**: (1) confrontation with disconfirming evidence, (2) critical reflection on assumptions, (3) rational discourse with others, (4) provisional action
- **Cannot be scripted, only designed for**: The curriculum builder can create conditions (present disconfirming case studies, structure peer dialogue, require assumption-testing exercises) but cannot guarantee the aha

### Critical Assessment

- Mezirow's theory is widely cited but empirically difficult to test — transformation is subjective and longitudinal
- The theory is strongest when applied to adult education contexts where learners bring established meaning perspectives
- Risk of trivializing: calling every interesting insight an "aha moment" deflates the concept
- The old template's approach (brainstorm 2-3 aha ideas per learning outcome) is precisely the kind of trivialization Mezirow's framework warns against

### Implications for Curriculum Builder

- Replace "aha ideas" with **"belief-challenging encounters"** — structured activities that surface and challenge existing assumptions
- Use Mezirow's phase 1 (disorienting dilemma) as a session design element: each module should include at least one case, scenario, or evidence set that challenges a common assumption
- Phase 3 (critical assessment of assumptions) maps to a specific activity type the builder should generate: "What did you assume before this module? What do you assume now? What changed?"
- The builder should NOT generate "aha moments" as content — it should generate the CONDITIONS for perspective transformation (disconfirming evidence + reflection prompts + peer dialogue structure)
