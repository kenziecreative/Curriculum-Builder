# Research Note: Peer Learning, Social Learning, and the Cohort Social Dimension
**Date:** 2026-03-15
**Researcher:** Agent research pass
**Purpose:** Evidence base for Phase 8 — what does research say about where learning happens in cohort-based intensive programs, and how much value comes from structured curriculum vs. peer interaction?
**Status:** First evidence pass across four research topics: peer learning in compressed formats, communities of practice in compressed timeframes, social constructivism applied to adults, and cohort effects on learning outcomes.

## How to Read This Note

Each major finding is tagged with one of five markers:
- **SUPPORTED** — evidence directly validates the claim
- **COMPLICATED** — evidence is real but creates conditions, limits, or nuances the doctrine does not acknowledge
- **CONTRADICTED** — evidence runs directly against a specific claim
- **EMERGING** — recent research is directionally supportive but not yet consolidated
- **GAP** — no solid evidence base found; claim is asserted, not established

# SECTION 1: COOPERATIVE AND COLLABORATIVE LEARNING — META-ANALYTIC EVIDENCE

## 1.1 — Effect Sizes: Cooperative vs. Individual Learning

Multiple meta-analyses converge on a consistent finding: cooperative/collaborative learning outperforms individual learning with effect sizes that are both statistically and practically significant.

**Key data points from meta-analyses:**

- **Johnson & Johnson (2002)** meta-analysis of 117 studies on the Learning Together method: effect sizes of **0.58 to 0.70** favoring cooperative over competitive and individualistic learning across achievement, socialization, motivation, and self-development. Hattie (2009) classifies these as "real world differences" at d > 0.40.
- **Kyndt et al. (2013)** meta-analysis of face-to-face cooperative learning: average weighted effect size for achievement was **ES = 0.54** (substantial); effect size for attitudes was **ES = 0.15** (smaller but pedagogically significant per Slavin's 0.20-0.25 threshold). Bowen (2000) found ES = 0.51 for undergraduates in STEM courses specifically.
- **Johnson, Maruyama, Johnson, Nelson & Skon (1981)** meta-analysis: **d = 0.78** effect size favoring cooperative methods — meaning the average student in a cooperative setting performs at the 78th percentile of students in individualistic settings. Cooperative vs. competitive learning: d = 0.03 (no meaningful difference between competitive and individual).
- **Slavin (1989)** best-evidence synthesis: cooperative learning was "clearly positive" in **72%** of comparisons, with only 15% favoring control groups and 13% showing no difference.
- **Hattie's Visible Learning database (2023 update):** scaffolding produces an effect size of **d = 0.82**, placing it among the top 10 of 252 measured influences. The EEF Teaching and Learning Toolkit rates collaborative learning at **+5 months additional progress**.
- **U.S. Department of Education meta-analysis (Means et al., 2010):** In online/blended contexts, collaborative instruction yielded effect size of **+0.25** and instructor-directed **+0.39**, while independent learning was only **+0.05** (not significant). "The type of learning experience moderated the size of the online learning effect."
- **Lou et al. (2001):** Small group learning had significantly more positive effects than individual learning with technology.

**Ability composition matters:** Low-ability students learn more in heterogeneous (mixed) groups. Medium-ability students benefit more from homogeneous groups. High-ability students perform equally well in either arrangement. This has direct implications for cohort composition design.

**Group size matters:** Research on collaborative activities shows performance does not increase for groups bigger than four. Cooperation decreases as group size increases. Three-member groups were more cooperative than seven-member groups (Chao et al., 2021).

**Finding [SUPPORTED — for cooperative learning superiority]:** The evidence across multiple decades of meta-analyses is unambiguous: cooperative learning produces substantially better outcomes than individual learning on achievement (d = 0.54-0.78), with additional benefits for motivation, self-esteem, and social skills. This is one of the most robustly supported findings in education research.

**Finding [CONTRADICTED — for the doctrine's individual-learner focus]:** The doctrine frameworks (TMA, DCR, Six Metaskills) are all framed as individual cognitive processes. The meta-analytic evidence says that learning outcomes are significantly better when structured socially. A curriculum builder that generates curricula optimized for individual learners is designing for the weaker condition (d = 0.05 in the Means et al. study for independent learning, vs. d = 0.25-0.39 for collaborative/instructor-directed).

- Kyndt et al. (2013), meta-analysis: https://motivatingus.files.wordpress.com/2017/03/kyndt-et-al-2013-edurev-cooperative-learning.pdf
- Slavin (1989), best-evidence synthesis: cited in https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1096789.pdf

## 1.2 — Conditions for Effective Cooperative Learning

Cooperative learning does not automatically produce positive results. The meta-analyses identify specific structural conditions:

**Johnson & Johnson's five essential elements:**
1. **Positive interdependence** — group success requires each member's contribution
2. **Individual accountability** — each person must demonstrate learning (prevents free-riding)
3. **Promotive interaction** — face-to-face encouraging, helping, supporting
4. **Interpersonal skills** — must be explicitly taught, not assumed
5. **Group processing** — groups reflect on their functioning and improve

**Slavin's critical conditions:**
- **Group rewards** (not individual rewards) combined with **individual accountability** (through task specialization or individual assessment) produce the strongest effects
- Without individual accountability, free-riding undermines outcomes
- Without group rewards, motivation to help peers diminishes

**Chen et al. (2018) CSCL meta-analysis warning:** Collaborative learning is "not a panacea" — design of the collaborative environment must be aligned with learning objectives, learning needs, and learning activities. Group awareness tools were the most valuable across all learning outcomes. Collaboration scripts were frequently used as instruction/guidance strategy.

**Finding [COMPLICATED — for curriculum builder design]:** A curriculum builder cannot simply add "group activity" instructions and expect collaborative learning benefits. The structural conditions (positive interdependence, individual accountability, group processing) must be encoded into activity design. This is a design constraint the builder must enforce, not a feature it can leave to facilitators.

**Sources:**
- Johnson & Johnson (1994, 2002): cited in https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1096789.pdf
- Slavin (1983, 1989): cited in https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9860218/
- Chen et al. (2018): cited in https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9860218/

# SECTION 2: COHORT-BASED LEARNING AND PEER RELATIONSHIPS

## 2.1 — Cohort Structure as Social Infrastructure

**Mauldin, Barros-Lane, Tarbet, Fujimoto & Narendorf (2022)** conducted a prospective mixed methods social network analysis of 70 MSW students in cohort-based education. Key findings:

- Cohorts are described as **"low-cost social environments"** because students frequently and repeatedly interact with relative ease
- Optimal cohort sizes in the literature: **10-26 students**, with structured schedules and high levels of interaction
- Cohort-based learning was introduced to higher education in the 1940s but became more common in the 1980s and continues to grow
- Cohort-based learning is designed to support **social integration** — it is an institutional characteristic, not a pedagogical method
- **Age effects:** Being younger is associated with greater social integration for undergraduates; evidence on graduate students' age and peer relationships is nearly nonexistent

**Diversity effect:** "Variations in the types and settings of graduate programs and the diversity within the student body affect students' peer relationships." Social work schools, which emphasize working with diverse populations, may produce different peer relationship patterns than other settings. This means cohort effects are context-dependent and not universally transferable.

**Finding [SUPPORTED — for cohort structure as learning infrastructure]:** Cohort-based learning functions primarily as social infrastructure that enables collaborative learning. The evidence treats it as a structural enabler, not a content delivery method. This supports the view that curriculum content and social structure are separate design layers, both of which must be intentionally designed.

**Finding [GAP — for cohort effects in compressed programs specifically]:** The Mauldin et al. study examines a full-semester cohort, not a compressed program. "There are few, if any, prospective mixed methods or quantitative studies that simultaneously examine individual-level factors and the impact of cohort membership on graduate students' social networks." The evidence base for compressed-format cohort effects is even thinner.

**Sources:**
- Mauldin et al. (2022): https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7102/12/3/205
- Frontiers cohort study: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2024.1457550/full

## 2.2 — Cohort Effects in Accelerator Programs

**Assenova & Amit (Wharton, 2024)** conducted the most comprehensive empirical study of accelerator program design and startup outcomes, using data from the Global Accelerator Learning Initiative: **23,364 applicants, 4,020 accelerated across 335 cohorts in 408 programs across 176 countries (2013-2019)**.

**Key findings on cohort composition:**

- **Knowledge-building peer interactions** are identified as a "primary design choice" — decisions about cohort structure, size, and diversity of peers
- Peer interactions play a **"pivotal role"** in building founders' knowledge: founders exchange best practices, share experiences, act as idea sounding boards, and contribute to **vicarious learning**
- Cohorts grant participants access to a **"reservoir of knowledge"** from other founders
- The study finds that accelerator participation is associated with positive effects overall, but design choices about cohort composition significantly moderate these effects

**Cohort composition design choices identified:**
- **Breadth vs. depth of knowledge** within cohorts (generalist vs. specialist)
- **Cohort size** varies widely: 8-15 startups (typical) to 100+ (large programs)
- Specialized cohorts align participants with shared goals and similar support needs, which "enhances network effects"
- Historically generalist, but increasing specialization driven by demand for specialized knowledge

**Meta-analysis finding (Springer 2025):** Accelerators' cohort-based structure, where ventures progress together rather than receiving individualized attention, "helps peer learning and shared experiences, allowing entrepreneurs to benefit from the collective knowledge and insights of their counterparts."

**Finding [SUPPORTED — for peer learning as primary value driver in compressed programs]:** In accelerator contexts (the closest analog to compressed professional programs), peer interactions are identified as a primary design element with direct effects on outcomes. The curriculum is one input; the cohort composition is another, and research treats them as comparably important design choices.

**Finding [COMPLICATED — for individual-focused curriculum design]:** The accelerator research frames program design as multi-dimensional: curriculum content, cohort composition, mentorship structure, and funding terms. A curriculum builder that only designs the content dimension is addressing one of at least four design layers that determine outcomes. The social layer (cohort composition, peer interaction structures) is not an add-on — it is a primary design variable.

**Sources:**
- Assenova & Amit (2024): https://faculty.wharton.upenn.edu/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/SMJ_3581_JQA.pdf
- Meta-analysis: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10961-025-10218-6
- Wharton Knowledge summary: https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/article/do-accelerators-improve-startup-success-rates/

# SECTION 3: COMMUNITIES OF PRACTICE AND COMPRESSED TIMEFRAMES

## 3.1 — Wenger's CoP Framework: Core Theory

Lave and Wenger (1991) define learning as "a process of participation in communities of practice, participation that is at first legitimately peripheral but that increases gradually in engagement and complexity."

**Three foundational elements of a CoP (Wenger et al., 2002):**
1. **Domain** — a shared area of interest that defines the set of issues
2. **Community** — people who care about the domain and interact around it
3. **Practice** — the shared repertoire of resources, experiences, tools, and ways of addressing problems

**Legitimate Peripheral Participation (LPP):** Newcomers begin at the periphery of a community, participating in simpler activities, and gradually move toward fuller participation. This is "not a structure, but a way of acting" (Lave & Wenger, 1991, p. 24). LPP is "an evolving relationship over the long-term." Participation entails both "talking within" (exchanging information necessary for ongoing activities) and "talking about" (stories and community lore).

**Critical theoretical point:** In this framework, learning IS social participation. It is not the acquisition of knowledge by individuals, but "the process by which learners were integrated into a knowledge community." The individual's learning is a byproduct of becoming a participant in a community, not the reverse.

**Finding [CONTRADICTED — for the doctrine's individual-learner framing]:** Lave and Wenger's framework directly inverts the doctrine's implicit model. The doctrine frames learning as individual cognitive development (Theory acquisition → Method application → Application practice; Deconstruct → Compare → Rebuild as individual analysis). CoP theory says learning IS the process of social participation. Individual skill development is a consequence of community integration, not a standalone process that happens to benefit from occasional group work.

## 3.2 — CoP Formation in Compressed Timeframes: The Time Problem

**Critical finding from the critical review (Smith, Byrd & Jaggars, 2017):** "In Wenger's own publications or in secondary sources that describe or interpret his theories, there is no clear-cut answer to the question of how much time is needed to arrive at a functioning CoP, but a general contention is that 'a shared repertoire cannot be rushed into existence' (Cousin & Deepwell, 2005, p. 61)."

**Duration evidence from reviewed studies:**
- Shortest reported: **6 weeks** (Moule, 2006)
- Short: **7 weeks** (Gray, 2004; Guldberg & Mackness, 2009)
- Medium: **10-12 weeks** (Brosnan & Burgess, 2003; Evans et al., 2014)
- Longest: **1 year** (Guldberg & Pilkington, 2006)
- **Critical gap:** "None of the aforementioned studies explored how time has contributed to the establishment of a functioning CoP."

**Wenger's stages of CoP development (adapted from Wenger 1998, Wenger et al. 2002):**

| Stage | Domain | Community | Practice | Institutional |
|-------|--------|-----------|----------|---------------|
| Potential | Undefined | Unconscious | Undeveloped | Unrecognized |
| Coalescing | Forming | Emerging | Developing | Bootlegged |
| Active/Maturing | Well defined | Energetic | Productive | Legitimized |
| Dispersed | Shifting | Slowing | Stagnating | Strategic |
| Transformation | Lost/changed | Dispersed | Archival | Transformative |

**Implication for compressed programs:** A 3-6 month accelerator or cohort program can, at best, move a group from "Potential" through "Coalescing" to the early edge of "Active." The "shared repertoire" that defines a functioning CoP — shared language, stories, tools, routines, concepts — takes time to develop. Compressed programs cannot produce mature CoPs. They can, however, create conditions for CoP formation if certain elements are in place.

**Wenger-Trayner (2022 guidebook):** Notes that CoPs provide both "short-term and long-term value. The focus on practice means that members solve immediate problems and learn immediately relevant skills. But over time these communities also build capabilities that have long-term effects." This suggests compressed programs can access immediate value but not long-term capability building through CoP formation alone.

**Finding [COMPLICATED — for compressed program design]:** Full CoP formation requires time that compressed programs do not have. However, CoP theory still offers design principles: shared domain definition, structured interaction around practice problems, and legitimate peripheral participation structures can be seeded in compressed formats even if they cannot mature within them. The curriculum builder should design for CoP seeding, not CoP completion.

**Finding [GAP — for empirical evidence]:** "Time remains an unexplored variable in online/blended learning research grounded in this theory." There is no empirical study that directly tests how much time is minimally required for CoP formation, nor any study specifically testing CoP formation in compressed professional programs.

**Sources:**
- Smith, Byrd & Jaggars (2017), critical review: https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1140262.pdf / https://olj.onlinelearningconsortium.org/index.php/olj/article/view/963
- Wenger-Trayner (2022), CoP guidebook: https://www.wenger-trayner.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/24-02-21-CoP-guidebook-second-edition-final-2.pdf

# SECTION 4: VYGOTSKY'S ZPD AND SOCIAL CONSTRUCTIVISM APPLIED TO ADULTS

## 4.1 — ZPD Beyond Children: Application to Professional Development

Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development — "the distance between the actual developmental level as determined by independent problem solving and the level of potential development as determined by problem solving under adult guidance or in collaboration with more capable peers" (Vygotsky, 1978, p. 86) — was originally developed for children's learning. Its application to adults requires examination.

**Shabani, Khatib & Ebadi (2010)** provide the most thorough treatment of ZPD in adult/professional contexts. Key findings:

- The ZPD was "originally developed to account for the learning potential of children" but has been investigated for teacher professional development
- For adults, the "more capable peers" framing is particularly apt — in professional contexts, peers with different expertise can scaffold each other across domains
- Multiple scaffolding mechanisms applicable to adult learners: **peer and mentor collaboration, action research, practicum experience, and professional discourse**
- The ZPD concept supports the importance of collaboration in professional learning: "the broad theoretical framework... is the use of ZPD as a tool for understanding the process of collaborative knowledge creation" (Kuusisaari, 2014)

**Critical nuance on scaffolding with adults:** Lave and Wenger (1991) critique the scaffolding metaphor as a "one-way communication process" where "the scaffolder constructs it alone and presents it to the novice." They contrast this with the ZPD's emphasis on "teacher-learner collaboration and negotiation as a bilateral process." For adult learners, the bilateral model is more appropriate — adults bring expertise and experience that make one-directional scaffolding inappropriate.

**Peer scaffolding beyond "more capable" peers:** The NYSED framework extends ZPD theory: "Learning is not limited to interactions between the learner and an adult or 'more capable' peer... When learners with relatively equal levels of knowledge work together they can create zones of proximal development for each other as their interactions give rise to ideas, which are then shared with peers and which are then further advanced and developed through collaboration" (Donato, 1994).

This is particularly relevant for professional cohorts where participants may have comparable overall expertise but different domain-specific knowledge — creating reciprocal ZPDs.

**Finding [SUPPORTED — for peer scaffolding with adults]:** ZPD applies to adults, but the mechanism shifts from expert-to-novice scaffolding to reciprocal peer scaffolding where professionals with different expertise create mutual zones of proximal development. This reciprocal model is better suited to cohort-based professional programs than the traditional unidirectional scaffolding model.

**Finding [COMPLICATED — for the curriculum builder's implicit model]:** The doctrine's TMA (Theory → Method → Application) implies a unidirectional knowledge transfer from curriculum to learner. Social constructivism says the most effective learning happens in the bilateral zone between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with peer support. A curriculum builder should design activities that create reciprocal ZPDs — activities where participants with different backgrounds scaffold each other — not just solo application exercises.

**Sources:**
- Shabani, Khatib & Ebadi (2010): https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1081990.pdf
- NYSED ZPD framework: https://www.nysed.gov/sites/default/files/programs/bilingual-ed/zone_proximal_development.pdf

## 4.2 — Collaborative vs. Individual Learning for Adults: The Evidence

**Rojas-Drummond & Mercer (2003):** Structured peer dialogue within the ZPD improved problem-solving performance by **40%** compared to unscaffolded groups.

**Johnson, Johnson & Smith (1998):** Meta-analysis focused specifically on **college settings and adults** found cooperative learning effects consistent with broader meta-analyses. The adult/professional context does not diminish the cooperative learning advantage — if anything, the reciprocal expertise effect strengthens it.

**RPPL Partnership (2025) position paper on professional learning:** "Research suggests that collaborative learning works in HQPL [high-quality professional learning] because it facilitates peer-to-peer interactions focused on improving instructional practices, fostering a supportive environment where teachers can learn from each other's experiences." However: "the field still has more to learn about how best to facilitate collaborative learning experiences that shift instructional practice. Many practice guides around PLCs highlight the need for particular routines, protocols, and processes with relatively little evidence of effectiveness."

**Key unresolved questions from RPPL:**
- How to sequence collaborative opportunities and balance individualized vs. group experiences
- Who should be in the room at which times
- The role of expertise — among peers and facilitators — in promoting effective peer learning

**Finding [SUPPORTED — for collaborative learning with adults]:** The cooperative learning advantage holds for adults and professionals. The effect sizes are comparable to those in K-12 contexts. Adult learners' existing expertise does not reduce the collaborative advantage — it creates additional channels for reciprocal scaffolding.

**Finding [GAP — for optimal collaborative design in compressed formats]:** While the superiority of collaborative over individual learning is well-established, the specific design features that optimize collaborative learning in compressed professional programs are under-researched. Questions about sequencing (individual first, then collaborative, or vice versa?), optimal group composition within cohorts, and facilitator role remain open.

**Sources:**
- Rojas-Drummond & Mercer (2003): cited in https://www.structural-learning.com/post/vygotskys-theory
- Johnson, Johnson & Smith (1998): cited in https://motivatingus.files.wordpress.com/2017/03/kyndt-et-al-2013-edurev-cooperative-learning.pdf
- RPPL (2025): https://rpplpartnership.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/RPPL_PositionPaper_041025_FINAL.pdf

# SECTION 5: COHORT COMPOSITION AND DIVERSITY EFFECTS

## 5.1 — Cohort Composition as Design Variable

The accelerator literature treats cohort composition as a first-order design variable. Assenova & Amit (2024) identify two dimensions:

**Breadth vs. depth of knowledge:**
- **Generalist cohorts** (diverse industries): maximize cross-pollination, novel combinations, serendipitous connections
- **Specialist cohorts** (same industry): align participants with shared goals, enhance network effects, enable deeper domain-specific peer learning
- The trend is toward specialization as domains mature

**Size effects:**
- Small cohorts (8-15): enable deeper relationships, higher interaction density, more personalized support
- Large cohorts (100+): enable greater diversity but dilute interaction quality
- Research on social dilemma problems: cooperation decreases as group size increases
- Optimal small group size for collaborative activities: **3-4 members** (performance does not increase beyond 4)

**Ability/experience composition:**
- Low-ability students learn most in **heterogeneous** groups
- Medium-ability students benefit from **homogeneous** groups
- High-ability students are indifferent to composition
- In professional contexts, "ability" maps to "expertise diversity" — the implication is that less experienced professionals benefit most from mixed-experience cohorts

**Finding [SUPPORTED — for cohort composition as design lever]:** Cohort composition (size, diversity, expertise distribution) directly moderates learning outcomes. This is not a logistical variable — it is a pedagogical one. A curriculum builder that ignores cohort composition is leaving a significant design lever unaddressed.

**Finding [COMPLICATED — for a tool that only designs content]:** If cohort composition significantly moderates outcomes, then the curriculum builder's intake process should capture cohort characteristics (size, experience distribution, domain diversity) and adapt activity design accordingly. Heterogeneous groups need different activity structures than homogeneous groups. This is a design constraint, not a facilitator preference.

# SECTION 6: SYNTHESIS — WHAT THE DOCTRINE MISSES

## 6.1 — The Social Dimension Gap

The doctrine's four frameworks (TMA, DCR, Six Metaskills, Flipped Classroom) are all fundamentally individual-learner models:

- **TMA** (Theory → Method → Application): describes an individual's cognitive journey from concept exposure to application
- **DCR** (Deconstruct → Compare → Rebuild): describes an individual's analytical process of taking apart, comparing, and reconstructing
- **Metaskills** (Exploring, Imagining, Creating, Adapting, Connecting, Feeling): five of six are individual cognitive/emotional capacities; Feeling captures empathy but not collaborative capacity
- **Flipped Classroom**: flips the individual's exposure to content but says little about what happens in the group-interaction phase beyond "do practice"

**What the evidence says is missing:**

1. **Social learning is not an add-on — it is the primary mechanism.** The meta-analytic evidence (d = 0.54-0.78 for cooperative vs. individual) says that social learning structures are among the most powerful pedagogical interventions available. Treating them as optional "group activities" misses their centrality.

2. **Peer interaction is not noise — it is signal.** In accelerator research, peer interaction is identified as a primary design element alongside curriculum content. A curriculum builder that designs content but not peer interaction structures is designing half the learning experience.

3. **Communities of practice reframe what "learning" means.** If learning IS participation in a community of practice, then curriculum content is a resource for community activity, not the activity itself. The builder should design for community activities that use content, not content that might include some community activities.

4. **Reciprocal scaffolding is the adult mechanism.** Adults don't need expert-to-novice scaffolding as much as they need reciprocal peer scaffolding where different expertise creates mutual ZPDs. Activity design should create these reciprocal zones.

5. **Structural conditions are non-negotiable.** Cooperative learning benefits require positive interdependence, individual accountability, and group processing. These are design constraints, not facilitator tips.

## 6.2 — Design Implications for the Curriculum Builder

A curriculum builder that encodes the doctrine's frameworks without a social learning layer will produce curricula optimized for the weaker learning condition (individual). The following structural features should be encoded:

**Per-activity social design requirements:**
- Activity type classification: individual, paired, small-group (3-4), full-cohort
- For collaborative activities: positive interdependence structure specified, individual accountability mechanism specified, group processing prompt included
- Reciprocal scaffolding design: what different expertise/perspectives do participants bring to this activity?

**Per-module peer interaction layer:**
- Peer feedback structures with specific protocols (not just "share with a partner")
- Cohort knowledge-sharing mechanism (what expertise exists in the cohort that this module should surface?)
- Community practice problem (a shared challenge the cohort works on together, not just individual application)

**Per-program cohort design inputs:**
- Cohort size and recommended sub-group sizes
- Expected expertise distribution (homogeneous vs. heterogeneous on which dimensions?)
- Progression from peripheral to fuller participation (early modules more structured, later modules more open)
- Community formation prompts (shared domain definition, shared practice establishment)

**Per-program validation:**
- At least 40-50% of learning activities should be collaborative (based on the meta-analytic evidence that collaborative outperforms individual)
- Every module should include at least one reciprocal scaffolding activity
- No module should be entirely individual-learner focused

## 6.3 — Caveats and Limitations

1. **Most cooperative learning research is on K-12 and undergraduate populations.** The adult/professional-specific evidence is thinner, though what exists shows comparable effects (Johnson, Johnson & Smith, 1998; RPPL, 2025).

2. **Compressed program effects are under-researched.** The CoP literature explicitly flags time as an unexplored variable. Whether cooperative learning effect sizes hold in 1-week vs. 12-week programs is unknown.

3. **The builder designs curriculum, not cohorts.** Cohort composition is a program design decision, not a curriculum design decision. The builder can encode cohort-aware activity design but cannot control who shows up. The intake process is the bridge.

4. **Implementation quality matters enormously.** As RPPL (2025) notes, "many practice guides around PLCs highlight the need for particular routines, protocols, and processes with relatively little evidence of effectiveness." Encoding collaborative structures is necessary but not sufficient — the structures need to be the right ones.
