# Cognitive Load, Session Pacing, and TMA at Session Level

**Research Questions**: (3) How does TMA function as a session-level design framework — sufficient alone, or needs complementary within-session engagement? (4) What does research on attention, cognitive load, and session pacing say about structuring adult learning sessions?

**Date**: 2026-03-15
**Finding Tags**: COMPLICATED (TMA at session level), SUPPORTED (cognitive load management), COMPLICATED (attention span claims)

## 1. Theory→Method→Application at Session Level

### How TMA Maps to Established Frameworks

TMA has direct analogs in established session-level design:

| TMA Stage | Gagné Events | 5E Phase | Merrill Principle | Gradual Release |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Theory | 1-5 (Attention through Guidance) | Engage, Explore, Explain | Activation, Demonstration | I Do |
| Method | 5-6 (Guidance, Elicit Performance) | Explain, Elaborate | Demonstration, Application | We Do |
| Application | 6-9 (Practice through Transfer) | Elaborate, Evaluate | Application, Integration | You Do |

### Is TMA Sufficient as a Session-Level Framework?

**Finding: COMPLICATED — TMA provides the macro arc but lacks critical within-session elements.**

What TMA provides:
- Clear progression from conceptual to procedural to applied
- Maps to Bloom's Taxonomy levels (Remember/Understand → Apply/Analyze → Create/Evaluate)
- Aligns with Gradual Release of Responsibility (Fisher & Frey, 2013)

What TMA does NOT provide at session level:
1. **No prior knowledge activation step** — Gagné Event 3, Merrill's Activation, 5E's Engage all start by connecting to what learners already know. TMA's "Theory" phase assumes a blank slate.
2. **No reflection/consolidation mechanism** — After Application, there's no structured reflection on what was learned, what assumptions changed, what transfers. Kolb's Reflective Observation phase is missing.
3. **No formative assessment checkpoints** — Gagné Events 6-7 (Elicit Performance, Provide Feedback) require checking understanding DURING the session, not just at the end.
4. **No attention management** — No mechanism for cognitive load pacing, activity switching, or the attention resets that sessions require.
5. **No transfer design** — "Application" in TMA means doing it in context; it doesn't address how to promote transfer to OTHER contexts (Gagné Event 9, Merrill's Integration).

### Recommendation: TMA + Bookends

TMA needs a **complementary within-session engagement pattern**. The minimum additions:

```
ACTIVATE → THEORY → CHECK → METHOD → PRACTICE → APPLICATION → REFLECT → TRANSFER
   ↑                    ↑                                          ↑         ↑
 (Added)             (Added)                                    (Added)   (Added)
```

- **Activate** (before Theory): Surface prior knowledge, create cognitive hooks
- **Check** (between Theory and Method): Verify conceptual understanding before procedural instruction
- **Reflect** (after Application): Structured reflection on what changed
- **Transfer** (final): Explicit connection to real-world contexts beyond the session

## 2. Cognitive Load Theory and Session Design

### Core Principles (Sweller, 1988-2024)

- **Working memory capacity**: 7±2 elements (Miller, 1956), but more recent research suggests 2-4 chunks for concurrent processing (Cowan, 2001)
- **Three types of cognitive load**:
  - *Intrinsic*: inherent complexity of the material (element interactivity)
  - *Extraneous*: load caused by poor instructional design
  - *Germane*: load devoted to schema construction (the productive kind)
- **Element interactivity** determines both intrinsic and extraneous load — high-interactivity content requires more working memory

### Session Design Implications

1. **Present new material in small steps** (Rosenshine Principle 2): Each step should introduce 2-4 new interacting elements, not more
2. **Worked examples first, then practice** (worked example effect): For novices, studying worked examples reduces cognitive load compared to problem-solving. For experts, this reverses (expertise reversal effect — Kalyuga & Sweller, 2003).
3. **Reduce split attention**: Keep related information spatially and temporally integrated. Don't make learners mentally combine information from separate sources.
4. **Manage redundancy**: Don't present the same information in multiple forms simultaneously (e.g., reading slides aloud verbatim adds load, not learning)

### The Expertise Reversal Effect at Session Level

**Source**: Kalyuga, S. (2007). Expertise Reversal Effect and Its Implications. *Educational Psychology Review*, 19(4)

This is critical for the curriculum builder because the tool must generate sessions for mixed-expertise audiences:

| Learner Level | Effective Strategy | Harmful Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Novice | Worked examples, structured guidance, high scaffolding | Open-ended problems, minimal guidance |
| Intermediate | Faded worked examples, some guided discovery | Full worked examples (redundancy) |
| Expert | Open-ended problems, productive failure, minimal instruction | Detailed step-by-step guidance (creates load) |

**Engine implication**: The structured intake (from Phase 5) captures learner expertise level. Session-level templates must adapt: novice sessions need more Theory/Method; expert sessions need more Application with minimal scaffolding.

## 3. Attention and Session Pacing

### The "10-15 Minute Attention Span" — Myth with a Grain of Truth

**Source**: Wilson, K. & Korn, J. H. (2007). Attention During Lectures: Beyond Ten Minutes. *Teaching of Psychology*, 34(2)

**Key finding**: Wilson & Korn reviewed studies of student note-taking, observations, self-reports, and physiological measures and found "little support for the belief that students' attention declines after 10 to 15 min."

**What the evidence actually shows**:
- Attention lapses occur much more frequently than every 10-15 minutes — at 30 seconds, 4-5 minutes, 7-9 minutes, and throughout
- Lapses are not a cliff; they're recurring micro-disengagements
- Individual differences are enormous and poorly accounted for in studies
- What matters is not session LENGTH but session VARIETY — switching activity types resets attention

### Practical Session Pacing Research

- **Bunce et al. (2010)**: Used clicker responses to measure attention during chemistry lectures. Attention lapses increased as the lecture progressed, but were significantly reduced when interactive activities were interspersed
- **Activity switching**: The most reliable attention reset is a change in modality or activity type (from listening to discussing, from individual to group, from conceptual to applied)
- **Cognitive load pacing**: High-interactivity content should be presented in shorter segments with processing breaks; low-interactivity content can sustain longer segments

### Spaced Practice and Interleaving Within Sessions

**Finding**: Interleaving (mixing different problem types) produces ~30% improvement in learning performance within a single session. More dramatic effects in physics: 50% improvement on test 1, 125% on test 2 (Nature, 2021).

Spacing is typically an across-session strategy (requires days/weeks between exposures). But interleaving works within a single session and has strong effects.

**Session design implication**: Practice activities should interleave different problem types rather than blocking same-type problems together.

## 4. What the Curriculum Builder Should Enforce at Session Level

### Required Session Elements (from research convergence)

1. **Prior knowledge activation** (first 5 minutes) — Every session must start by connecting to what learners know. Required field.
2. **Objective statement** — Learners must know what they'll be able to do. Required field.
3. **Chunked content delivery** — New material in 2-4 element chunks, not monolithic presentations. The builder should segment content with processing pauses.
4. **Activity type switches** — At least one modality change per 15-minute block. The builder should flag sessions with >15 minutes of same-activity-type.
5. **Formative check** — At least one understanding check before major practice. Required field.
6. **Guided practice before independent practice** — Gradual Release sequence. Required ordering.
7. **Reflection prompt** — Explicit reflection on what was learned and what assumptions changed. Required field.
8. **Transfer connection** — How does this apply beyond this session? Required field.

### Recommended but Not Required

- Interleaved practice activities (rather than blocked)
- Peer discussion/dialogue (transformative learning mechanism)
- Belief-challenging encounter (Mezirow's disorienting dilemma)

### What the Builder Should NOT Enforce

- Fixed session length (no evidence for optimal duration; depends on content and audience)
- Rigid phase timing (the "10 minutes per phase" approach has no empirical support)
- Single framework (different session types need different structures)
