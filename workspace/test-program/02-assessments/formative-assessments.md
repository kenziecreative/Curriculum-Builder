## FA-1: Statement Structure Quick Sketch
- **paired_objective:** SO-1-1-1
- **bloom_level:** Remember
- **format:** written
- **module_placement:** Module 1 / Session 1 (early)
- **instructions_for_learner:** Without looking at any reference materials, draw a simple outline of a cash flow statement. Label each of the three sections by name and write one sentence describing what kind of activity each section captures. You have 5 minutes.
- **success_criteria:** Correctly names all three sections (operating, investing, financing); each one-sentence description accurately reflects the type of activity captured in that section; no reference materials used.

## FA-2: Profit vs. Cash Oral Explanation
- **paired_objective:** SO-1-1-2
- **bloom_level:** Understand
- **format:** oral
- **module_placement:** Module 1 / Session 1 (mid)
- **instructions_for_learner:** You'll be given a one-paragraph scenario about a fictional business: they had a profitable quarter but their bank account is dangerously low. Explain to your partner — in plain language — why that can happen. You have 3 minutes to explain; your partner has 2 minutes to ask clarifying questions.
- **success_criteria:** Explanation correctly identifies that timing differences between invoicing and collection can create a gap between profit and cash; uses at least one specific mechanism from the scenario (e.g., unpaid invoices, early expenses); partner can paraphrase the explanation back accurately.

## FA-3: Cash Timing Reflection
- **paired_objective:** SO-1-1-3
- **bloom_level:** Understand
- **format:** reflective
- **module_placement:** Module 1 / Session 1 (close)
- **instructions_for_learner:** Think of a month in your own business when your income looked good on paper but your bank account felt tight — or vice versa. Write 2–3 sentences describing what happened and whether the timing of invoice payment (rather than invoice date) was part of the cause. If you can't recall a specific month, use the sample scenario provided.
- **success_criteria:** Written response correctly attributes the cash/income discrepancy to a timing difference (e.g., invoiced but not yet collected, or paid for supplies before billing the client); connects the explanation to their own or the sample business context; does not attribute the gap to profit alone.

## FA-4: Operating Section Line-Item Sort
- **paired_objective:** SO-1-2-1
- **bloom_level:** Apply
- **format:** performance-based
- **module_placement:** Module 1 / Session 2 (early)
- **instructions_for_learner:** You'll receive a printed or digital operating section from a sample cash flow statement with 10–12 labeled line items. For each line item: (1) mark it as "inflow" or "outflow," and (2) add a brief note explaining why it moves cash in that direction. When you're done, calculate the net operating cash flow by summing the inflows and subtracting the outflows.
- **success_criteria:** At least 10 of 12 line items correctly labeled as inflow or outflow; brief notes accurately describe the cash direction for at least 8 items; net operating cash flow calculation is accurate within $100 of the model answer.

## FA-5: Warning Sign Spotter
- **paired_objective:** SO-1-2-2
- **bloom_level:** Apply
- **format:** case-analysis
- **module_placement:** Module 1 / Session 2 (mid)
- **instructions_for_learner:** You'll receive two documents side-by-side: an income statement and a cash flow statement from the same fictional business, same month. Read both. Then answer three questions: (1) Is the business profitable this month? (2) Is operating cash flow positive or negative? (3) If you see a warning sign, circle it and write one sentence explaining what it means for the business's short-term health.
- **success_criteria:** Correctly identifies whether the business is profitable (yes/no, with the right number cited); correctly identifies whether operating cash flow is positive or negative; if the warning pattern is present, correctly identifies it and writes a sentence that accurately describes the solvency implication (not just "it's bad" — must explain the mechanism).

## FA-6: 90-Day Runway Calculator
- **paired_objective:** SO-1-2-3
- **bloom_level:** Apply
- **format:** performance-based
- **module_placement:** Module 1 / Session 2 (close)
- **instructions_for_learner:** Using the cash flow statement you've been working with today (your own, or the provided sample), calculate your 90-day cash runway using this formula: current cash balance ÷ average monthly operating cash outflow × 30. Show your work — write out each number you used, where you found it in the statement, and the final result. Then write one sentence interpreting what the number means: "I have approximately X days of runway."
- **success_criteria:** Formula is set up correctly with current cash balance and average monthly operating outflow; both input numbers sourced correctly from the statement (identified by section/line item); final calculation is within 10% of the model answer; interpretive sentence correctly translates the number into days of runway.

## FA-8: Two-Option Cash Impact Comparison
- **paired_objective:** SO-2-1-1
- **bloom_level:** Analyze
- **format:** performance-based
- **module_placement:** Module 2 / Session 3 (early)
- **instructions_for_learner:** Using your own cash flow statement (or the provided sample), work through two scenarios: (A) You delay your largest outstanding supplier invoice by 30 days — what does your 30-day cash position look like? (B) You send a collections follow-up to your three oldest unpaid client invoices and collect 50% within 30 days — what does your 30-day cash position look like? Show your calculations for both and write one sentence identifying which option produces a better 30-day cash position and by how much.
- **success_criteria:** Both scenario calculations set up with the correct inputs (identified from the statement); each 30-day cash position correctly calculated; conclusion correctly identifies which option produces a larger positive change; the margin between options is calculated accurately.

## FA-9: Credit Line Decision Threshold
- **paired_objective:** SO-2-1-2
- **bloom_level:** Analyze
- **format:** case-analysis
- **module_placement:** Module 2 / Session 3 (mid)
- **instructions_for_learner:** You'll receive three short business scenarios, each describing a cash gap situation with different constraints. For each scenario, decide: should this business accelerate collections, draw on their line of credit, or is there not enough information to decide? Write one sentence of reasoning for each decision that references at least one specific factor — cost, timing, or relationship risk.
- **success_criteria:** At least 2 of 3 scenario decisions are defensible given the data provided; each reasoning sentence names at least one specific factor (not just "it's better"); in scenarios where the answer is ambiguous, correctly identifies what additional information would resolve the decision.

## FA-10: Options Framework Card
- **paired_objective:** SO-2-1-3
- **bloom_level:** Analyze
- **format:** performance-based
- **module_placement:** Module 2 / Session 3 (close)
- **instructions_for_learner:** Complete the decision framework table below. Fill in all 9 cells: three rows (delay supplier payment, accelerate client invoicing, draw on line of credit) and three columns (typical cost to business, days until cash impact, relationship risk level — low/medium/high). ## FA-12: Scenario Decision Commitment
- **paired_objective:** SO-2-2-1
- **bloom_level:** Evaluate
- **format:** simulation
- **module_placement:** Module 2 / Session 4 (early)
- **instructions_for_learner:** You'll receive a realistic business scenario: a service business with a specific cash gap, two available options (one has been ruled out due to a stated constraint — no available credit line, or a supplier relationship that can't be risked), and a 14-day decision window. Select one option, commit to it (no hedging), and write one paragraph explaining your choice. Your paragraph must cite at least two specific numbers from the scenario data.
- **success_criteria:** Learner commits to a single option (no "it depends" answers without a commitment); rationale cites at least two specific data points from the scenario; reasoning demonstrates awareness of the rejected option's tradeoff (not just "I picked A" — must acknowledge why B was less suitable in this situation).

- **format:** performance-based

## FA-14: Decision Justification Draft
- **paired_objective:** SO-2-2-3
- **bloom_level:** Evaluate
- **format:** written
- **module_placement:** Module 2 / Session 4 (late)
- **instructions_for_learner:** Write a 2–3 paragraph justification of your cash decision — the one you committed to in the scenario work today. Your justification should: (1) state the decision clearly, (2) cite at least three specific figures from the cash flow statement that support it, and (3) explain what outcome you expect at 30 and 60 days. Write it as if you're explaining it to a business advisor who hasn't seen your statement.
- **success_criteria:** Decision stated explicitly in the first paragraph; at least three specific figures cited with their source (section/line item name); 30-day and 60-day projections included and consistent with earlier calculations; written clearly enough that a reader without the statement could evaluate the logic.

## FA-15: Module 2 Decision Scenario
- **paired_objective:** MO-2-2
- **bloom_level:** Evaluate
- **format:** performance-based
- **module_placement:** Module 2 / end of Session 4 (before SA-3)
- **instructions_for_learner:** Work through a complete decision scenario using a provided cash flow statement you haven't seen before. Complete all four steps: (1) identify the cash position and any warning signs, (2) name which of the three options are available to this business, (3) select one and justify it with specific figures from the statement, and (4) estimate how long your chosen action maintains solvency. You have 20 minutes.
- **success_criteria:** All four steps completed; at least one warning sign correctly identified if present; decision justified with at least two specific figures; solvency estimate is calculated (not guessed) and is within a reasonable range of the model answer.
