# Problem Requirements Document
> Generated from discovery interview on 2026-03-15

## 1. Problem Summary

Building curriculum that produces genuine behavioral change — not just knowledge transfer — requires pedagogical rigor that is structurally absent from current tools and workflows. The manual process of creating delivery-ready curriculum is so time-consuming that there is no bandwidth left for systematic evaluation, iteration, and improvement. The result: curriculum quality depends on who's in the room, review is ad hoc, and the transfer mechanisms that determine whether learning actually sticks are the first things sacrificed to time pressure.

## 2. Background & Context

Kelsey Ruger operates at the intersection of two organizations: **Kenzie Creative**, where he builds and delivers training programs for clients, and **Hello Alice**, where his team builds accelerator programs and webinar content for small business education. He has deep expertise in learning design, grounded in a pedagogical doctrine built around four frameworks: Theory-Method-Application (TMA), Deconstruct-Compare-Rebuild (DCR), the Six Metaskills, and the Flipped Classroom model.

Kelsey commissioned and completed an 11-phase research project (22 sources, 11 phases) that evaluated his existing accelerator prompt template against learning science standards. The research found the template was structurally biased toward marketing (60-65% of instructions) with pedagogy receiving 20-25%. More importantly, the research identified that the differentiator between effective and ineffective AI-assisted curriculum generation is **constraint architecture** — encoding pedagogical requirements as schema-enforced structural constraints rather than loose instructions the AI can interpret or ignore.

The convergence of three factors makes this the right time to build: AI technology is now capable enough to attempt this, the way people need to learn is shifting because of AI's impact on work, and the depth of research now possible means curriculum can be grounded in current evidence. Kelsey's evolving philosophy centers on focusing less on artifacts and more on goals and outcomes — letting AI handle the structural scaffold so the human can focus on the substance that determines whether someone actually learns.

## 3. Primary User

**Kelsey Ruger (Kenzie Creative)** — Deep pedagogical knowledge, understands learning science frameworks, builds and delivers training ranging from 90-minute sessions to full-semester courses. His bottleneck is not knowledge but time: the manual process consumes the bandwidth needed for evaluation and iteration. He wants to focus on content and context rather than structural assembly.

**Hello Alice Program Team (Secondary)** — Subject matter experts who know their content domains (small business education, accelerator curriculum, themed programs) but are not instructional designers by training. They need the tool to handle pedagogical rigor — Bloom's taxonomy, assessment alignment, scaffolding, transfer design — without requiring them to understand or specify those concepts. The intake process must ask questions an SME can answer using their domain knowledge, not instructional design vocabulary.

## 4. Current State

The current workflow is ad hoc. Whoever has the deepest domain knowledge takes the lead — sometimes Kelsey, sometimes a program manager. They use Claude conversationally to help think through and structure material, but it's a multi-step, manual process with no standardized framework applied across programs.

Review is peer-based: the program manager, the person delivering the training, and potentially Kelsey or another domain expert review the output. There is no consistent rubric, no systematic check for objective-assessment alignment, transfer design, or scaffolding quality. Quality depends on who's available and experienced enough to catch gaps.

The core pain is a capacity trap: manual curriculum development is so time-consuming that evaluation, iteration, and improvement get squeezed out. The team can't evaluate existing curriculum at depth, can't systematically improve programs over time, and can't ensure consistent quality across programs built by different people. The administrative lifting of structuring deliverables consumes time that should go toward the content decisions that actually determine learning outcomes.

## 5. Desired Outcome

**Creation speed:** Curriculum development is fast enough that systematic evaluation and iteration become part of the normal workflow, not a luxury.

**Consistent quality floor:** Every program that comes out of the tool meets the same pedagogical standard — complete objective-assessment alignment, Bloom's distribution, transfer design, social learning — regardless of who builds it.

**Evaluation at depth:** The ability to evaluate more curriculum, more rigorously, than is currently possible. Both new curriculum generated by the tool and existing curriculum assessed against the same standards.

**Behavioral change, not just knowledge transfer:** Programs produce genuine training outcomes — sustained behavioral change — through pre-program preparation, in-program application, and post-program entrainment mechanisms. The transfer ecosystem is not optional; it is the mechanism that makes everything else matter.

**Human focus on substance:** The tool handles the scaffold — structural rigor, assessment alignment, pedagogical framework compliance, deliverable formatting. The human handles the soul — content decisions, contextual relevance, the domain expertise that determines whether curriculum connects with learners.

**Observable success criteria:**
- Schema completion rate > 95% of required fields populated
- 100% objective-assessment alignment
- Bloom's level distribution across 4+ taxonomy levels per program
- Transfer design present in every module
- Marketing-to-pedagogy ratio < 25%
- All six metaskills activated with transfer prompts
- Preferred over existing template output on 6+ of 8 dimensions in blind expert evaluation

## 6. Constraints & Dependencies

- **Builder:** Kelsey, building with Claude Code as development environment
- **Platform:** Claude Code plugin (skills, subagents, hooks, state management)
- **Budget:** Self-built; no external development cost beyond Claude Code usage
- **Timeline:** No hard deadline. Real-world validation target: AccessU day-long training in May 2026
- **Output format:** Platform-agnostic markdown documents. Must not be locked to any LMS or delivery system. Adaptable to whatever delivery context is needed
- **User skill range:** Must be usable by both pedagogically sophisticated users (Kelsey) and subject matter experts without instructional design training (Hello Alice team)
- **Program duration range:** 90 minutes to full semester. The architecture must handle this range without requiring tool modifications per format
- **Existing research:** 11-phase research project with detailed design recommendations, schema specifications, validation protocols, and architecture decisions. This is the design foundation, not a starting-from-scratch build

## 7. Scope Boundaries

### In Scope (V1)

- Full nine-stage generation pipeline: Structured Intake, Outcome Design, Assessment Design, Module Structure, Session-Level Content, Metaskill Mapping, Transfer Ecosystem Design, Marketing Derivation, Full-Curriculum Validation
- TMA as the content arc within sessions (ACTIVATE -> THEORY -> CHECK -> METHOD -> PRACTICE -> APPLICATION -> REFLECT -> TRANSFER)
- DCR as the analytical method within sessions, with scaffolding for novices and validation step for wicked domains
- Six Metaskills operationalized as thinking routines (not labels), with developability hierarchy respected
- Social learning layer required per module (activity type, interdependence structure, accountability mechanism, group processing)
- Expertise-adaptive design keyed off intake (instructional strategy, session template selection, TMA ordering)
- Facilitator guides with timing cues, facilitation notes, common stumbling points, transition guidance
- Participant-facing materials: pre-work assignments, handouts, activity worksheets
- Presentation/slide framework outlines (structural, not final design)
- Transfer ecosystem: pre-program (readiness, manager briefing, baseline), in-program (implementation intentions, real-work application), post-program (spaced retrieval at 1/4/12 weeks, peer accountability, community continuation, evaluation design)
- Three-tier validation: automated schema checks, rubric-based scoring with confidence scores, human review surfacing
- Marketing materials derived from curriculum substance (program descriptions, learning promises, audience positioning)
- Schema-enforced constraints at every stage (not instructional suggestions)
- State management across sessions (STATE.md protocol)
- Parallel module generation via subagents
- Platform-agnostic markdown output

### Out of Scope (Future)

- Final slide/presentation design (structural outlines only in V1)
- LMS integration or platform-specific export formats
- Learner-facing delivery platform
- Real-time facilitation support during live training
- Automated learner assessment scoring
- Multi-language curriculum generation
- Video or multimedia content production
- Integration with specific project management or content management tools

## 8. Solution Recommendation

**Custom Application — Claude Code Plugin**

This is a purpose-built Claude Code plugin implementing a nine-stage generation pipeline with schema-enforced pedagogical constraints. This is the correct solution type because:

1. The constraint architecture IS the product. No existing tool encodes pedagogical requirements as structural generation constraints. Connecting existing tools or optimizing workflows cannot produce this.
2. The Claude Code plugin ecosystem directly supports the required architecture: skills (one per pipeline stage), subagents (parallel generation and validation), hooks (enforcement gates), state management, and reference file loading.
3. The research has already validated the platform fit and produced detailed design specifications. This is not speculative — it's executing on a thoroughly researched design.
4. The niche is unoccupied: 347+ Claude Code plugins, 1,900+ skills, zero for educational design.

## 9. Technical Direction

- **Platform:** Claude Code plugin (CLI-native)
- **Architecture:** Nine-stage sequential pipeline with parallel subagent generation within stages. Three-layer doctrine encoding: CLAUDE.md (operational rules, lean), schemas (binding constraints as required output fields), reference files (full doctrine, templates, rubrics — loaded on demand)
- **Key patterns:** Backward design as generation sequence. Schema-enforced required fields at every stage. Separate generation from validation (different subagents). State management via STATE.md for cross-session continuity
- **Plugin components:** Skills (one per stage + init + validate), subagents (module generator, session generator, validation agents), hooks (session-start context loading, pre-tool-use stage enforcement), reference files (doctrine, session templates, metaskill routines, validation rubric, schemas)
- **Session template library:** Gagne-derived (knowledge transfer), 5E/7E-derived (inquiry/exploration), Merrill-derived (problem-solving), WIPPEA-derived (professional development), Universal Arc with TMA overlay (default)
- **Key integrations:** None required. Output is platform-agnostic markdown
- **Technical constraints:** Must handle programs from 90 minutes to full semester without architectural changes. Context management critical — full curriculum can exceed 100k tokens; subagent composition prevents context exhaustion
- **Known technical risks:**
  - Validation quality for scaffolding and pedagogical appropriateness requires human judgment — automated scoring has documented limits
  - LLM intra-rater consistency is poor for qualitative judgment; validation agent must produce confidence scores, not pass/fail
  - No existing holistic scoring instrument for AI-generated curriculum; composite rubric must be constructed from QM, TALQ, backward design, and Hu et al. dimensions

## 10. Minimum Lovable Product (MLP)

The first version produces a **complete, delivery-ready curriculum package** from a single structured intake conversation. A subject matter expert at Hello Alice should be able to answer the intake questions using their domain knowledge, and receive back a curriculum that meets pedagogical standards they didn't have to specify — with facilitator guides they can actually use to deliver the training, participant materials ready for learners, and a transfer ecosystem that extends the program's impact beyond the session itself.

The experience should feel like working with an expert instructional designer who asks the right questions, handles all the structural rigor, and hands you back everything you need to deliver a program that produces behavioral change — not just a pleasant learning experience.

What V1 does NOT do: it does not produce final visual designs, it does not deliver content to learners directly, and it does not automate facilitation. It produces the complete intellectual and structural architecture of a training program as documents that a skilled facilitator can pick up and run with.

## 11. Open Questions & Gaps

- **Imagining as teachable metaskill:** No consolidated instructional evidence base exists across 11 research phases. The tool activates it through adjacent practices (scenario planning, futures thinking, mental simulation) with appropriate evidence-gap framing. Whether this is sufficient remains unvalidated.
- **DCR as integrated sequence:** Each component validates separately with strong evidence. No studies test DCR as a named integrated framework. The tool encodes a coherent theoretical synthesis, not a proven protocol.
- **Cooperative learning at compressed durations:** Meta-analytic evidence (d = 0.54-0.78) is primarily from multi-week courses. Whether these effect sizes hold for 90-minute or half-day programs is unknown.
- **Minimum viable CoP formation time:** The transfer ecosystem includes community continuation design, but no research establishes minimum time for community of practice formation in compressed formats.
- **The integrated nine-stage pipeline is untested as a system.** Each stage is evidence-grounded, but the combined sequence is a design proposal. The AccessU training in May 2026 will be the first real-world test.
- **SME usability:** The intake process must be answerable by subject matter experts without instructional design vocabulary. Whether the structured intake achieves this requires testing with the Hello Alice team.
- **Facilitator guide depth:** What level of facilitation guidance is sufficient for an SME delivering a program they didn't design? This may vary by program complexity and facilitator experience.
- **Metaskill social dimension:** The research identified that the Six Metaskills omit social/interpersonal dimensions present in every comparable framework. A decision is needed: expand the taxonomy to seven, or reframe the six as intrapersonal capacities with collaborative activation. This is a doctrine decision, not a tool architecture blocker.

## 12. Business Model Notes

This is currently a professional tool for internal use across Kenzie Creative and Hello Alice, not a commercial product. Revenue impact is indirect:

- **Kenzie Creative:** Faster, higher-quality curriculum delivery for client engagements. Competitive differentiation through pedagogically grounded output that demonstrably exceeds industry standard.
- **Hello Alice:** Consistent quality across accelerator programs regardless of which team member builds the curriculum. Ability to produce and evaluate more programs at depth. Scalable curriculum development without proportional headcount scaling.
- **Future commercial potential:** The research identified that the Claude Code educational design niche is completely unoccupied (zero plugins out of 347+). If the tool proves its thesis, there is a potential product opportunity — but that is not the current goal and should not influence V1 design decisions.
