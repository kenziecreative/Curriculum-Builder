# Conversion Copywriting Doctrine

Reference file for marketing copy generation. Load this before writing any marketing output.

Source: Hello Alice conversion copywriting training (Confluence).

---

## The "You Rule"

Every sentence should address the reader directly. Rewrite sentences to begin with "you," "your," or variations. The reader cares about themselves — not the program, not the curriculum, not the framework. Brand/program name goes below the fold, after the reader sees themselves in the copy.

**Transform this:**
> "This program teaches structured AI delegation frameworks."

**Into this:**
> "You'll walk away knowing exactly which tasks to hand to the AI and which to keep human."

## Structural Frameworks

Use one of these frameworks as the backbone for every piece of marketing copy. Choose based on the audience's emotional state.

### PAS (Problem → Agitate → Solution)

Use when the audience has a pain point. Name the problem, make it feel urgent, then resolve it.

```
PROBLEM: You've used AI for accessibility work — and watched it ship errors nobody caught.
AGITATE: Every update breaks your prompts. Every new project is a blank slate. You're rebuilding from scratch each time, and the stakes are too high for trial and error.
SOLUTION: This program gives you the structural layer that survives tool changes — frameworks you build once and carry forward.
```

**Rules:**
- Don't rush past the agitation. The emotional connection drives the decision.
- The solution must directly address the problem stated — not a tangentially related benefit.

### DOS (Desire → Obstacle → Solution)

Use when positioning toward an aspiration rather than a pain.

```
DESIRE: You want to use AI confidently for accessibility work — hand off the mechanical tasks and focus on judgment calls.
OBSTACLE: But right now, every workflow is improvised. There's no structure to catch errors before they ship, no way to know what's safe to delegate.
SOLUTION: In one day, you'll build that structure — specification artifacts, review commands, and a personal delegation framework you can use on any project.
```

### PCPO (Problem → Cure → Proof → Offer)

Use for longer-form body copy where you need proof elements.

```
PROBLEM: Your AI accessibility workflows break every time the tool updates.
CURE: Build delegation frameworks based on work structure, not tool features — they survive any tool change.
PROOF: Practitioners who use structural delegation frameworks report consistent output quality across 3+ tool transitions. (cite specific source)
OFFER: One-day hands-on workshop. You leave with working artifacts, not notes.
```

### FAB (Feature → Advantage → Benefit)

Use for describing specific program elements within longer copy.

```
FEATURE: Five-question durability classification framework
ADVANTAGE: Sorts any piece of AI guidance into principle, heuristic, or tactic in under a minute
BENEFIT: You stop wasting time on recommendations that won't survive the next tool update
```

**Template:** [Feature] lets you [Advantage], which means [Benefit].

## Writing for Clarity

These rules apply to ALL marketing output.

### Sticky, Not Smooth

Copy should grab attention, not slide past. If the reader's eyes can skim without stopping, the copy is too smooth.

**Smooth (bad):** "Leverage innovative AI frameworks to enhance your accessibility workflow efficiency."
**Sticky (good):** "Your AI workflow breaks every time the tool updates. This fixes that."

### Kernel Sentences

Structure: "Blank is Blank." State direct relationships. Keep it simple.

**Complex:** "Utilize our comprehensive framework to evaluate the durability of AI-generated accessibility recommendations across multiple tool transitions."
**Kernel:** "This framework tells you which AI advice will last."

### Precise Language

Replace vague terms with specific ones. Numbers beat adjectives.

| Vague | Precise |
|-------|---------|
| "takes a long time" | "takes three hours" |
| "save money" | "save $300" |
| "a comprehensive framework" | "a five-question framework" |
| "many participants" | "24 practitioners" |
| "improve your workflow" | "cut your review time from 45 minutes to 10" |

### Remove Fluff

Cut these on sight:
- Warm-up sentences ("In today's rapidly evolving landscape...")
- Clever wordplay that sacrifices clarity
- Unnecessary modifiers ("very," "really," "quite," "innovative," "cutting-edge")
- Throat-clearing ("It's worth noting that...", "As you may know...")

## Headline Formulas

Generate multiple headline options using these formulas. Pick the strongest.

### Problem-Solution
- "Who Else Wants [Most Desirable Outcome]?"
- "Now You Can [Desirable Thing] [Counter to Expectations]"
- "Here's the [Best Adjective] Way to [Solve a Problem]"

### Time-Based
- "[Do Something Hard] in [Period of Time]"

### Blank for Blank
- "[Product/Outcome] for [Specific Person] who [Specific Situation]"

### Blank Unlike Any Blank
- "[This Program] Unlike Any [Category]"

**Example applications for curriculum marketing:**
- "Who else wants to use AI for accessibility work without worrying about what ships?"
- "Now you can delegate to AI agents without rebuilding your workflow every update"
- "Design durable AI workflows in one day"
- "AI workflow training for accessibility practitioners who are tired of starting from scratch"

## Voice of Customer (VOC)

Marketing copy must use the language learners actually use to describe their frustrations — not how instructional designers describe competencies.

**How to apply VOC:**
- Describe problems using the words the target audience would use in conversation
- "I keep rebuilding my prompts from scratch" not "practitioners experience workflow discontinuity"
- "I don't know what's safe to hand off" not "delegation tier uncertainty persists"
- Benefits should sound like what someone would say after the program: "Now I know exactly what to delegate" not "participants demonstrate structured delegation competency"

**Stage-appropriate messaging:**
- Early-stage prospects (haven't decided to enroll): Lead with benefits. Answer "Why should I show up?"
- Mid-stage (comparing options): Lead with specific capabilities and differentiators
- Late-stage (ready to commit): Lead with logistics, proof, and risk reduction

## Seven Sweeps — Post-Generation Quality Check

After generating marketing copy, run these seven checks in order. Each is a quick pass, not a rewrite.

### 1. Clarity Sweep
Is everything immediately understandable? No jargon, no ambiguity, no sentences that require re-reading.

### 2. Voice & Tone Sweep
Is the tone consistent throughout? Does it sound like a confident colleague, not a brochure? Does it match the curriculum-voice.md baseline?

### 3. "So What?" Sweep
For every sentence: would the reader care? Is the value obvious? If a sentence doesn't answer "so what does this mean for me?", cut it or rewrite it.

### 4. "Prove It" Sweep
Every claim must trace to something the curriculum actually delivers. No unsourced promises. If you can't ground it, cut it.

### 5. Specificity Sweep
Replace every vague term with a concrete one. Numbers, timeframes, specific outcomes. "You'll learn frameworks" → "You'll build a five-question classification framework and test it on your own project."

### 6. Heightened Emotion Sweep
Does the copy create emotional resonance? Not manipulation — authentic connection to the reader's real frustration or aspiration. The agitation in PAS should feel real, not academic.

### 7. Zero Risk Sweep
What would stop someone from enrolling? Address objections: "What if my AI tool is different?" "What if I'm not technical enough?" "Will this still apply next year?" Reduce friction.

## Anti-Patterns — Never Do These

- **Source references in copy.** Traceability lives in the file's traceability table, never inline with prose.
- **Schema field names as labels.** No "Learning Promises" as a section header — use "What You'll Be Able to Do" or "What Changes."
- **Curriculum structure as selling point.** Nobody enrolls because the program has "5 modules with formative assessments." They enroll because they'll walk away able to do something they can't do today.
- **Passive benefit statements.** "Participants will be exposed to..." is not marketing. "You'll build..." is.
- **Generic promises.** "Gain valuable skills" means nothing. "Build a delegation framework you can use on Monday" means everything.
- **Smooth, skimmable prose.** If the reader can skim without stopping, the copy isn't doing its job.
